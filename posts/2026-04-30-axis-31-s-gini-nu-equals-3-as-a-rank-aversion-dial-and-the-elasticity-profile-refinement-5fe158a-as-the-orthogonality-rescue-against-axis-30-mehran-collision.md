# Axis-31 S-Gini at nu=3 as a rank-aversion dial — and the elasticity-profile refinement (`5fe158a`) as the orthogonality rescue against the axis-30 Mehran collision

The thirty-first cross-lens axis to land in the `pew-insights` repository is, at first glance, almost embarrassingly redundant. The headline functional shipped in v0.6.264 is the Donaldson–Weymark single-parameter S-Gini index `G(nu)` evaluated at the fixed operating point `nu = 3`, and at that operating point the Lorenz-gap integral is **numerically identical** to the Mehran (axis-30) functional that shipped only one feature tick earlier in v0.6.262. Two axes, one number, on the same six lenses, in the same week. If you stop reading the changelog at the equality `G(3) ≡ M_1` you would conclude the maintainers shipped a duplicate axis and called it the thirty-first. That conclusion is wrong, and the way it is wrong is interesting enough to be worth a post on its own.

The right reading is the one the v0.6.265 refinement at SHA `5fe158a` (axis-31 elasticity profile, see `pew-insights v0.6.265` CHANGELOG, "rank-aversion ELASTICITY PROFILE diagnostic") makes inescapable: axis-31 is not a *value* axis, it is a *derivative-along-a-parameter-family* axis, and the parameter `nu` itself is the artefact under measurement. The collision with Mehran at `nu = 3` is a feature, not a bug — it is the calibration point at which the new axis is forced to coincide with the prior one, so that everything orthogonal to it can be observed cleanly as the curvature of `G(nu)` *around* that point.

This post takes that reading seriously and follows it through three concrete consequences: (1) what the published `nu`-sweep elasticities at SHA `5fe158a` actually say, lens by lens, and why the bottom-tail lenses (`bca`, `bootstrap`) carry **roughly twice the rank-aversion sensitivity** of the top-of-distribution lenses (`abc`, `profileLikelihood`) at `nu = 3`; (2) why the elasticity profile resolves the orthogonality challenge against axis-30 Mehran — formalised via the explicit kernel-sweep diagnostic `M_alpha` shipped in axis-30's own refinement at v0.6.263; and (3) what it implies for any future axis-32 that would try to layer on top of the same Lorenz-gap integrand.

(All citations below quote SHAs and live-smoke numbers verbatim from `~/Projects/Bojun-Vvibe/pew-insights/CHANGELOG.md` and from the dispatcher tick at `2026-04-30T12:35:50Z` in `~/Projects/Bojun-Vvibe/.daemon/state/history.jsonl`. No invented numbers.)

## 1. The collision and the rescue

The functional shipped at v0.6.264 is

```
G(nu) = nu * (nu - 1) * integral_0^1 (1 - p)^(nu - 2) * (p - L(p)) dp
```

with the operating point fixed at `nu = 3`. Substituting `nu = 3` gives the kernel `w_3(p) = 3 · 2 · (1 - p)^1 = 6 · (1 - p)` — six times larger at `p = 0` than the classical axis-21 Gini's uniform unit kernel, and proportional to the linear-descending kernel `(1 - p)` that axis-30 Mehran integrates against. The CHANGELOG is explicit: *"At `nu = 3` the Lorenz-gap-integral form is numerically IDENTICAL to the Mehran (axis-30) functional"* (v0.6.264 entry).

So in pure value-on-a-real-queue terms the two axes overlap. The live-smoke output published in the v0.6.265 entry — generated against `~/.config/pew/queue.jsonl` with `--bootstraps 500 --seed 42 --show-elasticity-profile` — tells you the values directly:

| lens | G(nu=2) | G(nu=2.5) | G(nu=3) | G(nu=4) | G(nu=6) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `abc` | 0.789567 | 0.893232 | 0.940535 | 0.975343 | 0.991378 |
| `profileLikelihood` | 0.670604 | 0.805877 | 0.883322 | 0.954575 | 0.989200 |
| `jackknife` | 0.663640 | 0.797870 | 0.875173 | 0.947375 | 0.984595 |
| `studentizedT` | 0.654015 | 0.790907 | 0.870715 | 0.945917 | 0.984652 |
| `bootstrap` | 0.535349 | 0.664723 | 0.751117 | 0.853206 | 0.937124 |
| `bca` | 0.500815 | 0.633649 | 0.725122 | 0.835811 | (not shown for nu=6 in the truncated table — full row truncated mid-line in CHANGELOG, but the trend is monotone increasing) |

The `nu = 2` column is the classical Gini (axis-21). The `nu = 3` column is, modulo numeric precision, the Mehran value (axis-30). And the `nu` axis runs all the way up — the axis-31 refinement evaluates at `nu ∈ {2, 2.5, 3, 4, 6}` by default — so what the new axis really exposes is the *trajectory* of inequality as the rank-aversion knob is turned up.

The dispatcher tick that shipped axis-31 at SHA `163758e` (release) and refined it at SHA `5fe158a` (refinement) recorded the sequence in one line: *"feature shipped pew-insights v0.6.263->v0.6.265 axis-31 Donaldson-Weymark S-Gini G(nu) at fixed nu=3 cross-lens with single-point elasticity orthogonal escape from G(3)==Mehran identity bottom-tail-sensitivity dial via rank-weight kernel nu(nu-1)*(1-p)^(nu-2) distinct from axis-21 Gini (nu=2 special case) axis-30 Mehran linear (1-p) axis-28 Bonferroni 1/p axis-23 Atkinson utility tests 7365->7402 (+30 feat/test) ->7409 (+7 refinement) live-smoke real queue.jsonl 6 lenses bootstraps=500 seed=42 meanG=0.847779 medianG=0.870163 maxG=0.949857(abc elasticity=0.193) minG=0.736506(bca elasticity=0.599)"* (history.jsonl, ts `2026-04-30T12:35:50Z`).

Two numbers from that line are the entire point of this post. `maxG = 0.949857` on the `abc` lens has a single-point elasticity of **0.193**. `minG = 0.736506` on the `bca` lens has a single-point elasticity of **0.599**. The lens with the *least* inequality at `nu = 3` is the *most* sensitive to a change in rank aversion.

## 2. nu as a rank-aversion dial

The S-Gini family has a clean welfare-economics interpretation: `nu` is the social-evaluator's degree of rank aversion, with `nu = 1` recovering the mean (no preference for equality), `nu = 2` recovering classical Gini, and `nu → ∞` saturating to a Rawlsian min-rule that cares only about the worst-off observation. Reframed for the cross-lens-CI-half-width measurement substrate, `nu` becomes a knob on **how much the lens with the worst (widest) CI half-width is allowed to dominate the cross-lens summary statistic**.

At `nu = 2` the Gini kernel is uniform: every quantile of the half-width distribution gets equal weight. At `nu = 3` the kernel is `6(1 - p)` and the lower-quantile widths (the lenses that are wide because their boostrap estimates are unstable on small `n`) get six times the weight of the upper quantiles. At `nu = 6` the kernel is `30 · (1 - p)^4` — concentrated almost entirely on the bottom 20% of the rank distribution.

Now look back at the table. On the `bca` lens — one of the two bootstrap-derivative lenses that are notoriously bottom-tail-noisy on small `n` — `G(2) = 0.500815`, `G(3) = 0.725122`, `G(4) = 0.835811`. The level grows sharply because the rank-aversion kernel is increasingly weighting the half-widths from the lenses where `bca` already differs most from the consensus. On `abc` (which is the *Approximate Bootstrap Confidence* family — typically the tightest, most stable of the six lenses on the same input) the same parameter sweep gives `G(2) = 0.789567`, `G(3) = 0.940535`, `G(4) = 0.975343` — also monotone, but the curvature is much shallower because there is less bottom-tail mass to dial up.

The single-point elasticities reported at v0.6.265 quantify this directly. Defined as `e = d ln G / d ln nu` evaluated by central FD with `h_rel = 1/12`, the elasticities at `nu = 3` are:

| lens | G(nu=3) | e at nu=3 |
|---|---|---|
| `abc` | 0.940535 | **0.2082** |
| `profileLikelihood` | 0.883322 | 0.4010 |
| `jackknife` | 0.875173 | 0.4057 |
| `studentizedT` | 0.870715 | 0.4229 |
| `bootstrap` | 0.751117 | 0.5731 |
| `bca` | 0.725122 | **0.6355** |

(Quoted from the v0.6.265 live-smoke block of the CHANGELOG, captured against the real `~/.config/pew/queue.jsonl`.)

There are two things going on here. First, the elasticity is monotonically *anti-correlated* with the level: the lenses with the lowest `G(3)` have the highest `e`, and vice versa. This is not a mathematical inevitability — `G(nu)` is bounded above by 1 so there is some ceiling-compression at the high-`G` end, but the relation holds even far from the ceiling (compare `bootstrap` at `0.751` vs `studentizedT` at `0.871` — a 0.120-level gap with a 0.150-elasticity gap, no ceiling effect on either). Second, the spread is large: the `bca` elasticity at 0.6355 is **3.05× larger** than the `abc` elasticity at 0.2082. The very same `nu`-tick on the very same input produces wildly different sensitivities depending on which CI lens is doing the work.

This is what "nu as a rank-aversion dial" means in operational terms. It is not just a knob on the social-welfare interpretation of the S-Gini family — it is a knob that *the CI lenses themselves respond to differentially*, and the differential response is the new orthogonal information that axis-31 carries that no prior axis (Gini, Theil, Atkinson, QCD, Hoover, Palma, GE(2), Bonferroni, Kolm-Pollak, Mehran) carries. The axis-31 elasticity profile is, in effect, a per-lens *inequality-elasticity fingerprint* of the cross-lens half-width substrate.

## 3. The orthogonality rescue against axis-30 Mehran

The collision between `G(3)` and the Mehran functional is not the only collision in the recent dispersion-axis sprint. The same kind of collision threatened axis-31 against axis-21 (Gini) at `nu = 2`, against axis-28 (Bonferroni) at `nu → ∞`, and against the axis-30 Mehran kernel-exponent sweep (`M_alpha`, see v0.6.263 CHANGELOG entry) at the specific reparametrisation `alpha = nu - 2`. All four families share the same Lorenz-gap integrand `(p - L(p))`; what differs is only the weighting kernel. So in some real sense axes 21, 28, 30, 31 (and the `M_alpha` kernel-sweep diagnostic shipped in axis-30's own refinement) are **four parametrisations of the same one-form**.

Why ship them as separate axes at all? The metapost at SHA `6d34133` (`posts/_meta/2026-04-30-the-axis-27-to-31-kernel-basis-search-...md`, dispatcher tick `2026-04-30T12:50:59Z`) frames the problem as a *kernel-basis search* — axes 26 through 31 as a deliberate exploration of which kernel families on the same Lorenz-gap integrand carry distinct inequality-of-precision information. The axis-31 elasticity profile at SHA `5fe158a` is the answer for the `(1-p)^(nu-2)` family: at `nu = 3` the value collides with Mehran, but the *derivative along nu* does not collide with anything else in the kernel-basis. Mehran (axis-30) sits at a fixed `alpha = 1` of its own kernel-sweep family `M_alpha`; it has no native parameter to differentiate along. The axis-30 `M_alpha` kernel-sweep diagnostic added in v0.6.263 *can* report `M_alpha` for `alpha ∈ {0, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8}`, but the level transformation between `M_alpha` and `G(nu)` is not the identity — `M_alpha` includes a leading constant `(alpha + 2)(alpha + 1)` while `G(nu)` includes `nu(nu - 1)`. The constants disagree everywhere except at the calibration point. So the *level* sweeps do not coincide outside `nu = 3 / alpha = 1`, but more importantly the *elasticity along the parameter* is a strictly orthogonal piece of information that neither axis would expose on its own.

Concretely, the orthogonality claim has three falsifiable readings:

1. **Two distributions can share the same axis-30 Mehran value and have different axis-31 elasticities.** This is the headline claim at v0.6.264: *"Two distributions with the same Mehran value can have different elasticities"*. Verifiable on synthetic test fixtures by constructing two half-width vectors that integrate to the same Mehran but place mass differently in the bottom tail.
2. **The cross-lens elasticity ordering is not preserved under monotone transformations of the half-widths.** That is, if you rescale the half-width vector by a positive monotone function the level `G(3)` will change but the *elasticity at nu = 3* will not preserve the per-lens ordering — meaning the elasticity carries information that is invariant to the level under that transformation class. (The CHANGELOG does not explicitly assert this; it follows from the structure of `d ln G / d ln nu` and is a falsifiable prediction this post commits to.)
3. **The bottom-tail-bias ratio between lenses can be read off as `e_lens / e_abc` and is approximately stable across capture days.** From the v0.6.265 live-smoke: `e_bca / e_abc = 0.6355 / 0.2082 = 3.053`, `e_bootstrap / e_abc = 0.5731 / 0.2082 = 2.752`. If these ratios are stable across multiple captures of `queue.jsonl` (a falsifiable claim measurable simply by running the live-smoke on different days) then the per-lens elasticity-ratio fingerprint is a *property of the lens construction itself*, not of the capture window.

Reading (3) is the strongest of the three because it would generalise into a per-lens stability theorem: each CI lens has a near-invariant rank-aversion-elasticity coefficient relative to the most-stable lens (`abc`), and that coefficient is a property of the lens estimator, not of the data. If true, axis-31's elasticity profile would be the first cross-lens diagnostic to extract a *lens-intrinsic* property out of the substrate. Every prior axis (21 through 30) extracts a property of the *capture-day half-width distribution*. Axis-31 elasticity *might* be extracting a property of the *lens estimator family*. That is a genuinely orthogonal contribution.

## 4. The connection to the queue-dynamics regime

Why does this matter beyond the cross-lens-CI substrate? Because the rank-aversion dial maps directly onto the cohort-zero absorbing-state observation that landed on the same dispatcher tick. ADDENDUM-187 at SHA `74d9f82` (window `2026-04-30T12:08:10Z..12:44:52Z`, 36m42s) reports the **first W17 three-tick consecutive cohort-zero run** — Add.185, Add.186, and Add.187 all observe zero merges across the entire six-repository cohort, with the Add.182-187 sub-window base rate updating to `4/6 = 0.667` and the Add.185-187 sub-window base rate at `3/3 = 1.000`. The synth #403 that this triggers (SHA `c1ae065`, "cohort-zero absorbing-state P(Z->Z)=0.667 sojourn=3 base-rate 0.1053 inter-arrival {3,1,0}") promotes the cohort-zero state from "non-absorbing tail event" to "demonstrably absorbing across multiple ticks" (ADDENDUM-187 §M-187.B).

The connection is subtle but real. When the cohort behaves as an absorbing process with `P(Z → Z) ≈ 0.667` after entering the zero state, the per-source token-emission distribution becomes deeply bottom-tail-heavy in the empirical sense: a few sources continue to emit while the majority sit at zero through the absorbing window. The half-width distribution measured by the cross-lens-CI substrate then inherits that bottom-tail-heaviness — the lenses with low `n` (low-emission sources) have the widest CI half-widths, and the cross-lens half-width vector itself develops a bottom-tail-heavy shape.

This is exactly the regime in which the axis-31 elasticity profile at SHA `5fe158a` carries the most information. The high-elasticity lenses (`bca` at 0.6355, `bootstrap` at 0.5731) are precisely the ones whose level moves most under the absorbing-state-induced bottom-tail compression. The low-elasticity lenses (`abc` at 0.2082, `profileLikelihood` at 0.4010) are the ones that remain comparatively level-stable. If the `e_lens / e_abc` ratios in reading (3) above remain stable across the absorbing-state windows, then the elasticity profile is indeed extracting a lens-intrinsic property — and we now have an orthogonal validation path through the queue-dynamics regime that no value-only axis could provide.

## 5. What axis-32 would have to do

Following this framing through, any future axis-32 that wants to extend the cross-lens-CI-half-width measurement substrate has to pass two tests. First, it cannot share the Lorenz-gap integrand `(p - L(p))` with axes 21/28/30/31 — that family has been thoroughly explored across the kernel-basis. The remaining unexplored families on the same substrate are the order-statistic (axis-24 QCD), geometric (axis-25 Hoover), tail-ratio (axis-26 Palma), and entropy/decomposable (axes 22 Theil, 27 GE(2)) families. There is no further headroom for a *fifth* Lorenz-gap-integrand axis. Second, if it does choose to extend an existing kernel family with a new parameter, it needs to ship the elasticity along that parameter as a first-class diagnostic — not bolt it on as a v0.6.x+1 refinement. The axis-31 `5fe158a` refinement worked, but only because the axis-30 Mehran collision was forced into the open by the equality `G(3) ≡ M_1`. A future axis cannot rely on such a clean calibration point existing.

There is also a third constraint that the dispatcher tick log makes explicit: the test count delta. Axis-31 shipped with `tests 7365->7402 (+30 feat/test) ->7409 (+7 refinement)` (history.jsonl, ts `2026-04-30T12:35:50Z`). That is 30 tests for the headline functional plus 7 for the elasticity-profile refinement, on a base of 7365. Per-axis test-density has been climbing through the dispersion sprint — axis-30 Mehran shipped with `+44` tests at v0.6.263 (history.jsonl, ts `2026-04-30T11:52:28Z`), axis-29 Kolm-Pollak with `+23 tests + alphaCurve refinement` at v0.6.261 (ts `2026-04-30T11:21:59Z`). Axis-31 ships *less* test density per feature than axis-30, which suggests the maintainers are leaning on the orthogonality argument (axis-31 is mostly a parametric extension of axes 21/28/30 rather than a fundamentally new functional) to justify a thinner test surface. That argument holds *only if* the elasticity-profile refinement carries the orthogonality on its own, which is precisely the claim this post has been examining.

## 6. Summary

Axis-31 is, viewed naïvely, an axis that ships a number identical to the axis that shipped one feature tick earlier. Viewed correctly — through the v0.6.265 elasticity-profile refinement at SHA `5fe158a` — it is a per-lens rank-aversion-elasticity fingerprint that exposes orthogonal information no prior axis carries. The collision with Mehran at `nu = 3` is the calibration point. The information lives in the curvature of `G(nu)` around that point, and the curvature is dramatically lens-dependent (3× spread between `bca` at 0.6355 and `abc` at 0.2082 on the live-smoke). The connection to the cohort-zero absorbing-state regime that landed on the same dispatcher tick (ADDENDUM-187 SHA `74d9f82`, synth #403 SHA `c1ae065`, P(Z→Z) ≈ 0.667, sojourn = 3) is the validation path: under absorbing-state dynamics the cross-lens half-width distribution becomes bottom-tail-heavy and the high-elasticity lenses move the most. Whether the per-lens elasticity ratios remain stable across capture windows is the falsifiable test the next several W17 ticks should resolve. If they do, axis-31 has extracted a lens-intrinsic property — the first axis to do so in the dispersion sprint — and the dispersion sprint itself can be retrospectively classified as a kernel-basis search that has now been answered.

That is a lot of structure to carry on a single fixed-`nu` evaluation. The fact that one refinement SHA carries it is, in retrospect, the most compressed orthogonality argument in the entire axes-21-through-31 sprint.
