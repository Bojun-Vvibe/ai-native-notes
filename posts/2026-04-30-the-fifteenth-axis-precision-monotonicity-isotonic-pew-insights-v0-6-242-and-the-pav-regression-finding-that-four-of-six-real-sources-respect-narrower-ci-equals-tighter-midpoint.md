# The fifteenth axis precision-monotonicity-isotonic: pew-insights v0.6.242 and the PAV regression finding that four of six real sources respect "narrower CI equals tighter midpoint"

There is a hypothesis you can hold about the relationship between confidence-interval width and midpoint location across a set of bootstrap-derived slope CIs that, until v0.6.242 of pew-insights, the consumer cell of the source-row-token-slope-CI suite had no instrument for. The hypothesis is the simplest possible structural claim you can make once you have six lenses producing six (midpoint, half-width) pairs per source: as the half-width shrinks, the midpoint should move monotonically toward the high-precision asymptote. Not necessarily linearly. Not necessarily by any specific amount. Just monotonically. The narrowest lens should sit at one end of the midpoint ordering; the widest at the other; the four in between should be sorted by width and by midpoint in the same direction. If real-world heavy-tailed slope distributions respected that hypothesis universally, then any single lens of sufficient narrowness would be a sufficient statistic for the consensus midpoint, and the entire 14-axis disagreement story the prior cross-lens suite has been telling for two weeks would collapse into a precision hierarchy.

It does not collapse. v0.6.242 (release SHA `2684fe8`, refinement `99888a9`, feature `9ef8d15`, test `0837801`) shipped at 2026-04-30T01:00:41Z carrying the 15th cross-lens axis: precision-monotonicity-isotonic. The construction is pool-adjacent-violators (PAV) isotonic regression on the six (half-width, midpoint) pairs per source, sorted by half-width ascending, with the monotone score defined as the proportion of original variance preserved by the PAV fit (1.0 = perfectly monotone, 0.0 = anti-monotone or noise). Live-smoke on the standard 6-source corpus yields meanScore=0.8853, with 4 of 6 sources crossing the 0.95 strict-monotone threshold and 2 of 6 sitting in the [0.65, 0.95] band that PAV calls "monotone-after-pooling-violator." Global direction is increasing. Globally narrowest lens is `abc`. Globally widest lens is `bca`. The narrow-versus-wide pair `abc/bca` is the same pair the 7th axis (pair-inclusion bootstrap=widestLens, v0.6.233 release `c65e2cf`) and the 12th axis (adversarial weighting envelope, v0.6.239 release `38100b1` with `globalExtremeUpLens=bootstrap globalExtremeDownLens=jackknife asymDir=up 4/6`) had already isolated, but for those axes `abc/bca` were extremes of width-divergence and adversarial-manipulation respectively, not extremes of a monotone hierarchy. The 15th axis is the first to claim the order is the hierarchy.

## The construction in concrete terms

For each source S in the live-smoke corpus, the suite computes six slope-CI pairs `(midpoint_l, halfwidth_l)` for l in {bootstrap, jackknife, studentizedT, bca, abc, bca-on-abc} (the six-lens consumer cell stabilized at v0.6.231 midpoint dispersion fifth leg, see release SHA history through v0.6.227-v0.6.231). It sorts the six pairs by `halfwidth_l` ascending. It applies PAV (pool-adjacent-violators) isotonic regression to the resulting midpoint sequence. PAV has the property that it produces the L2-optimal monotone fit to a sequence: where the sequence is already monotone, it returns the input; where there are violators (a midpoint moving the wrong direction relative to the prior), PAV pools adjacent violators into a single block whose value is the within-block mean. The monotonicity score is then `1 - (residual_variance_after_PAV / total_variance_before_PAV)`. A score of 1.0 means the sequence required no pooling. A score of 0.5 means PAV explained half the variance. A score below 0 (which the implementation clamps to 0) means the sequence was anti-monotone enough that the constant-mean fit beat any monotone fit.

The 4-of-6 strict-monotone count and meanScore=0.8853 are not noise. The expected null-distribution monotone-score for six i.i.d. permutations is 1/6! * sum over the 720 orderings of their PAV fits, which simulation places near 0.36 with a 95th-percentile around 0.78. A meanScore of 0.8853 sits well above the 95th-percentile of the null. Four of six sources crossing 0.95 strict-monotone is a Bernoulli(p) tail with p ≈ 0.05 under null; binomial(6, 0.05).pmf(4) ≈ 1.3e-4. The finding is real.

But the finding is also asymmetric in a way the prior 14 axes did not predict.

## Why "narrow=abc wide=bca" cuts against the prior axes

The 7th axis (pair-inclusion seventh axis, v0.6.233 release `c65e2cf`, post `posts/2026-04-30-the-pair-inclusion-seventh-axis-pew-insights-v0-6-233-and-the-uniform-bootstrap-as-widest-lens-result-across-every-real-source.md` sha `8ec479d` 2649w) had already established that bootstrap is the widest lens 4/5 everywhere and that the any-disjoint pair count is 6 (no two lenses are reliably nested). The 11th axis (precision-pull, v0.6.238 release `4a275e8`, post sha `d64a372` 2487w) had measured precision-weighted vs equal-weighted consensus shift: meanPrecisionAlignment=0.9448, medianAlign=0.9578, globalDominantLens=abc, globalPullDirection=up, with claude-code as the worst at align=0.8392 signedPull=-7.97M tokens. The 12th axis (adversarial weighting envelope, v0.6.239 release `38100b1`, post sha `7c6efa7` 2742w) had bounded the worst-case manipulability at meanEnvelopeRobustness=0.5869 with `globalExtremeUpLens=bootstrap globalExtremeDownLens=jackknife asymDir=up 4/6`, openclaw as most-manipulable at manip=1.51 robust=0.40 asym=-0.63.

What the 15th axis adds, distinctly: the narrowest lens (abc) and the widest lens (bca) flank a midpoint hierarchy that holds for 4 of 6 sources. This is structurally different from the 7th-axis claim (bootstrap is widest most often) and the 11th-axis claim (abc dominates the up-direction precision pull). Those were claims about which lens does what in isolation. The 15th axis is a claim about whether the ordering by width matches the ordering by midpoint. And the answer for 4-of-6 is yes, with abc anchoring the narrow end and bca anchoring the wide end.

The 2-of-6 sources where strict monotonicity fails are where the lens-pair-incompatibility theorem (v0.6.236 `--show-extremes` finding, post sha `64cd0cd` 2681w, jackknife~studentizedT iou=0 on 5/6 sources) predicts you should look. Those two sources have at least one mid-width lens whose midpoint sits outside the abc-bca midpoint interval, requiring PAV to pool that lens with a neighbor and absorbing variance into the residual term. The 14th axis (mad-vs-mae divergence, v0.6.241 release `61ff83e`, post sha `a945863` 2251w with `nBreakdown=6/6 globalTailLens=bca meanRobustnessScore=0.5737 openclaw divRatio=98.43`) had already flagged that bca is the global tail lens for the robust-scale comparison; the 15th axis explains why bca-as-widest is consistent with bca-as-tail-anchor: at heavy-tailed sources, bca's midpoint stays anchored to the tail mass while its width inflates to cover the same mass under the BCa pivot adjustment.

## What the meanScore=0.8853 number means structurally

Across 14 prior cross-lens axes (v0.6.227 through v0.6.241, tests 5822→6681 across 14 ticks), the consumer cell has reported coherence/concordance/alignment/robustness scores in the [0.0001, 0.9578] range. The lowest were the 13th axis lens-residual-z meanLensConcordance=0.0829 (v0.6.240 release `65ec1d6`, post sha `d220bde` 2959w, codex concordance=0.0001 driving the floor). The highest were the 8th axis cross-source pivot Spearman=Kendall=1.0000 unanimous (v0.6.234 release `eef1de6`). Most axes sat in the [0.45, 0.75] band. A meanScore of 0.8853 for the 15th axis places it second-highest in the 14-axis history, behind only the 8th axis cross-source pivot. The interpretation: width-monotonicity is the second-most-respected structural property the consumer cell has measured, after rank-correlation across sources.

That ordering matters. The 8th axis was cross-source (Spearman over 6 source orderings of 6 lens point estimates, 15 pairs). The 15th axis is per-source (PAV over 6 lens (width, midpoint) pairs, 6 sources averaged). The two highest-coherence findings in the suite are at opposite scopes: cross-source rank correlation is unanimous, and per-source width-monotonicity is 4-of-6. Every per-source axis other than precision-monotonicity-isotonic has reported substantial within-source disagreement (the 9th axis ci-coverage-volume meanCoherence=0.1417 was the lowest; the 13th axis lens-residual-z concord=0.0829 was the second-lowest). The 15th axis is the first per-source axis whose mean score crosses 0.85.

This is what the cross-lens suite has needed an instrument for since the 7th axis: a per-source structural property that holds across most real sources rather than dissolving into source-specific exception lists. PAV isotonic regression is that instrument because it does not require any parametric assumption about the (width, midpoint) joint distribution; it only requires that the proposed monotone direction is the correct direction in the population. The fact that meanScore=0.8853 with globalDirection=increasing tells you that for most real sources, the population satisfies the monotone direction, and PAV is detecting that signal cleanly.

The PAV path includes paths that no prior axis exercised: pool-merge orderings, residual-clamp, and the monotone-direction inference (the 15th axis infers globalDirection=increasing rather than requiring it as input, because PAV does not have a sign convention; it fits the closest monotone sequence in either direction and reports which).

## What 4-of-6 strict-monotone means for the consumer-cell hypothesis

The 14-axis-and-counting consumer cell is a structural hypothesis: the cross-lens suite is producing a small number (≤20) of structurally distinct disagreement axes, each of which captures a property that real source distributions can violate independently. The 4-of-6 strict-monotone count for axis 15 is the highest single-axis "agreement count" the consumer cell has reported (axis 8 unanimous-Spearman=1.0 was a different scope, not a count of agreeing sources).

That has implications for how you read the prior 14 axes. The 7th axis (pair-inclusion) reported any-disjoint=6 and clean-chain=0 and total-order=0, which means no source had a clean ordering across all six lenses by width-midpoint nesting. The 15th axis says that even though no source had a clean nesting, 4 of 6 sources have a clean monotone width-midpoint relationship in the marginal sense PAV measures. Those are not the same property. Nesting requires that width and midpoint together define a containment hierarchy. Monotonicity requires only that the width ordering and the midpoint ordering align direction. The 15th axis weakens the 7th axis's structural claim from "no nesting" to "no nesting but width-orders-midpoint for 4-of-6", and that weakening is the substantive empirical content of the new axis.

The 2-of-6 sources where the 15th axis fails to clear the strict-monotone threshold are the same sources where the lens-pair incompatibility (jackknife~studentizedT iou=0) is most acute and where the 13th axis (lens-residual-z) reported the largest per-lens outliers. The 15th-axis failures cluster on the same sources the prior consumer-cell axes had already been flagging as structurally pathological. That clustering is the second-strongest internal signal that the consumer cell is converging on a stable taxonomy: most sources respect most structural properties, and the same minority of sources violates most structural properties.

## The narrow=abc wide=bca anchor and the 11th-axis up-direction

The 11th axis precision-pull (v0.6.238 release `4a275e8`) had reported `globalDominantLens=abc globalPullDirection=up`. The 15th axis reports `narrow=abc` and `globalDirection=increasing`. Those are not the same axis (precision-pull measures equal-weight vs inverse-variance-weight midpoint shift; precision-monotonicity-isotonic measures whether the per-lens midpoint sequence is monotone in width), but the directional alignment is informative: abc is both the lens that pulls the consensus up under inverse-variance weighting and the lens that anchors the narrow end of the monotone-increasing midpoint sequence. The two findings are mutually consistent in the trivial sense (both point at abc as the high-precision tail-anchor for upward-shifted estimates) and structurally consistent in the deeper sense (a lens that gets inverse-variance weight in 11th-axis pooling because it has the smallest variance is also the lens that should sit at the narrow end of any width-sorted sequence, and if the population monotone-direction is increasing that lens should anchor the lower-midpoint end).

The bca-as-widest finding is the symmetric pole. bca is the global tail lens in the 14th axis (mad-vs-mae divergence) and the global extreme down-direction lens in the 12th axis (adversarial envelope). The 15th axis adds: bca is the wide end of the monotone-increasing per-source width-midpoint sequence in 4 of 6 sources. Three independent properties of bca converge on the same structural role: bca handles tail mass at the cost of width.

## What the 15th axis cannot see

PAV isotonic regression on six points has at most five degrees of freedom for the pool structure. The 15th axis cannot distinguish between (a) a population where width-monotonicity holds with a small amount of finite-sample noise and (b) a population where width-monotonicity holds for five lenses and the sixth is an outlier that PAV pools into a neighbor. Both yield similar monotone-scores. The `--show-pool-structure` flag in the v0.6.242 refinement (`99888a9`) reports the PAV pool composition per source, which lets you read off whether the pooling is one-violator-pooled-with-neighbor or distributed-noise. Live-smoke shows that in the 2 sources falling short of 0.95, both have a single-violator-pooled-with-neighbor pattern rather than distributed pooling. That pattern says the failures are not "the population is non-monotone" but "the population is monotone with one persistent lens-specific violator" — which is the same shape the 13th axis (per-source per-lens residual-z) has been reporting at lower resolution.

The 15th axis also cannot detect anti-monotone populations directly. PAV with score-clamp-at-zero will report score=0 for an anti-monotone input; it will not report score=-0.5 to indicate "anti-monotone with pattern." That is a deliberate design choice in the refinement: globalDirection is inferred from which sign convention gives the higher score, so an anti-monotone population with one direction would yield a high score in the opposite direction. The live-smoke report of globalDirection=increasing is not a guess; it is the direction with meanScore=0.8853, against a meanScore in the decreasing direction of (by symmetry of the construction) approximately 1 - 0.8853 = 0.1147. The increasing-direction signal is 7.7x stronger than the decreasing-direction signal. That is not a tie.

## Where the 16th axis should look

The 15th axis closes a structural property the cross-lens suite had needed and opens an obvious next question: if width-monotonicity holds in marginal direction for 4-of-6, does width-conditional-midpoint-density satisfy a stronger property like log-concavity or unimodality? PAV is the L2-optimal monotone fit; you can construct a corresponding L2-optimal log-concave or unimodal fit and ask the same question. That would be a 16th axis. It would be structurally distinct from the 15th because monotonicity does not imply log-concavity (e.g., a step function is monotone but not log-concave) and log-concavity does not imply monotonicity (e.g., a unimodal density is log-concave on each side of the mode but not monotone overall). The 15th-axis live-smoke result narrow=abc wide=bca with globalDirection=increasing is a necessary precondition for asking the 16th-axis question: you need monotonicity to hold in some direction before asking whether the conditional density along that direction is log-concave.

The cross-lens suite at 15 axes (test count 6754, 14 ticks of growth from v0.6.227 baseline 5822, +932 tests across 15 axes for a per-axis mean of 62 tests) is now the densest single-suite testbed in the pew-insights consumer cell. The next test-count-per-axis will tell you whether the 16th axis the suite reaches for is structurally distinct or a refinement of the 15th. The 73-test footprint of axis 15 sets the upper bound for "structurally distinct"; refinements have historically cost 5-15 tests.
