# Session Duration Percentile Cliff: The P90→P95 39× Jump

There is a particular shape that shows up when you measure agent session
durations across a fleet of CLI tools, and it is not a power law in the
clean textbook sense. It is something nastier: a long, well-behaved body
that suddenly falls off a numerical cliff somewhere between the 90th and
95th percentile, after which the tail does not decay so much as it
simply continues at a flat, enormous magnitude until you run out of
samples.

Today's queue snapshot makes the shape unusually crisp. Across 8,509
sessions in `~/.config/pew/session-queue.jsonl`, the median session is
51 seconds long. The 90th percentile is 1,102 seconds — call it
eighteen and a half minutes. Then the 95th percentile jumps to 43,352
seconds. That is not a typo and it is not a parsing artifact: it is just
over twelve hours. Between p90 and p95 the curve climbs by a factor of
roughly thirty-nine. Between p95 and p99 it merely doubles.

I want to spend this post on the mechanics of that cliff, why it is not
the same thing as a heavy tail in the usual sense, what it does to any
SLO you might try to write against "session duration," and why
percentile thresholds chosen at sane-sounding spots like p90 will lie to
you about this fleet specifically.

## The numbers

I ran the following query at `2026-04-26T02:43:24Z` against the
session queue (`wc -l` reports 8,509 lines):

```
jq -s '
  map(select(.duration_seconds != null and .duration_seconds >= 0)) as $all
  | ($all | length) as $n
  | ($all | map(.duration_seconds) | sort) as $s
  | {
      n: $n,
      min: $s[0],
      p10: $s[($n*0.10|floor)],
      p25: $s[($n*0.25|floor)],
      p50: $s[($n*0.50|floor)],
      p75: $s[($n*0.75|floor)],
      p90: $s[($n*0.90|floor)],
      p95: $s[($n*0.95|floor)],
      p99: $s[($n*0.99|floor)],
      max: $s[-1],
      mean: (($all | map(.duration_seconds) | add) / $n)
    }
' ~/.config/pew/session-queue.jsonl
```

Verbatim output:

```
{
  "n": 8509,
  "min": 0,
  "p10": 0,
  "p25": 10,
  "p50": 51,
  "p75": 206,
  "p90": 1102,
  "p95": 43352,
  "p99": 87426,
  "max": 1730983,
  "mean": 5466.738159595722
}
```

A few things deserve a slow look.

First, the bottom of the distribution is collapsed. The 10th percentile
is zero seconds. So is the minimum. That is not a bug; it is the
fingerprint of a class of session that opens, posts a snapshot row, and
closes within the resolution of the duration field. It is also not a
small minority — at p10 = 0 we are saying that at least 850 of the 8,509
sessions registered as duration-zero. The 25th percentile is ten
seconds. Three quarters of all sessions are gone before the median
human has finished reading the first sentence of an agent reply.

Second, the body of the distribution from p25 to p90 spans a factor of
about a hundred (10s → 1,102s). That is heavy but not monstrous; it is
within the range you would expect from any system where a few sessions
happen to need a long-running agent loop and most do not.

Third — and this is the part nobody anticipates the first time —
between p90 and p95 the value multiplies by 39.3. Between p95 and p99
it goes up by a factor of about 2.0. Between p99 and the max it goes up
by another factor of ~19.8. The tail is not smooth. There is a literal
cliff edge somewhere around the 91st percentile, above which the
behaviour is qualitatively different from anything below it.

The mean lands at 5,466 seconds, roughly 91 minutes. Half of all the
sessions in the corpus are shorter than 51 seconds, and the average
session is an hour and a half. That ratio — mean/median ≈ 107 — is
itself a tell. In a log-normal you would expect mean/median in the low
single digits even for fairly fat distributions. Mean/median > 100
basically requires the tail to be doing all the work.

## Why percentile cliffs happen in agent telemetry

If you came up through web latency monitoring you have an instinctive
prior that p90 and p95 are "neighbours." You will quote both numbers in
the same breath in a postmortem, and most of the time the second one is
maybe 1.5× to 3× the first. That intuition is calibrated against
distributions where the underlying generative process is roughly
homogeneous — every request is the same shape, just with variance.

Agent session durations are not generated by one process. They are
generated by at least three:

1. **Probe sessions.** Editor integrations, snapshot pings, "is the
   agent alive" handshakes, sessions that exist only to record that
   something happened. Duration ≈ 0–10 seconds. This is the bottom of
   the distribution and it is enormous in row count.

2. **Working sessions.** A human types a prompt, an agent does a
   thing, a human reads the result, types the next prompt, eventually
   stops. Duration ≈ tens of seconds to maybe twenty minutes. This is
   the body. The p25–p90 region is essentially this class.

3. **Long-running loops.** Either a multi-hour autonomous run, or a
   session that was opened, never explicitly closed, and is still being
   considered "open" by the snapshotter because the process is alive
   and has issued an event within whatever the timeout window is.
   Duration ≈ many hours to many days.

What you are observing when you draw the percentile curve is not a
single distribution with a heavy tail; it is the union of three
distributions whose supports barely overlap. The cliff between p90 and
p95 is exactly the seam between class 2 and class 3. It is very sharp
because almost nothing actually lives in the gap between "twenty
minutes" and "many hours" — those are categorically different uses of
the tool, executed by categorically different operators (one of them is
often not a human at all), and the smooth-curve assumption breaks.

## By source: where the cliff lives

To check the multi-process hypothesis I split the same query by
`source` field. Same data file, same timestamp:

```
[
  {"source": "opencode",    "n": 4910, "p50": 41,  "p90": 388,   "p99": 90861, "mean": 1974},
  {"source": "openclaw",    "n": 2013, "p50": 122, "p90": 65733, "p99": 99021, "mean": 16025},
  {"source": "claude-code", "n": 1108, "p50": 43,  "p90": 604,   "p99": 34407, "mean": 3818},
  {"source": "codex",       "n": 478,  "p50": 61,  "p90": 372,   "p99": 9159,  "mean": 690}
]
```

The sources are not behaving the same way at all.

`openclaw` is the obvious outlier and it is the one driving most of the
fleet-wide cliff. Its p90 is already 65,733 seconds — over eighteen
hours. Its median is only 122 seconds, which means below the 90th
percentile it looks like a perfectly normal interactive tool, but
somewhere past p90 a different population takes over and that
population is sustained multi-day loops. The p90:p99 ratio for
`openclaw` is just 1.5; the cliff has effectively already happened by
p90. Inside this source there is no working-session vs long-loop seam
visible at the percentile boundaries my query happens to use, because
the long-loop population is enormous enough to start at p90 and never
stop.

`opencode` looks the way I expected the whole fleet to look: median 41
seconds, p90 388 seconds (~6.5 minutes), p99 90,861 seconds (over a
day). The cliff between p90 and p99 is a factor of 234. So `opencode`
also has the seam, but the seam sits between p90 and p99, not at p95.
Its working-session class extends much further into the upper
percentiles than `openclaw`'s does.

`claude-code` is similar in shape to `opencode` but milder:
median 43, p90 604, p99 34,407. The p90:p99 ratio is 57. Cliff
present, less violent.

`codex` is the cleanest, most well-behaved source by this metric.
Median 61, p90 372, p99 9,159, mean 690. The p90:p99 ratio is 25, which
is still high by web-services standards but is the smallest in the
fleet. Notably, `codex` also has the smallest sample (478 sessions), so
some of this could be that the long-loop class has not had a chance to
accumulate yet — but the per-source mean of 690 seconds is roughly an
order of magnitude lower than every other source, which suggests the
shape is real and not just sample-size luck.

## Means lie, percentiles lie differently

The fleet mean of 5,466 seconds (91 minutes) is, in the strict
arithmetic sense, true. As a description of any actual session you
might encounter it is wrong. The mean is closer to the 92nd percentile
than to the median. If you ever see a dashboard reporting "average
session duration: 91 minutes" for this fleet and you do not also see
the percentile breakdown, the dashboard is lying to you by accident.

Per-source means tell the same story but more honestly because the
means are no longer averaging across qualitatively different
populations. `codex` mean 690 seconds is dominated by working sessions
plus a thin tail. `openclaw` mean 16,025 seconds is dominated by the
long-loop tail and the working sessions are essentially noise floor.

The percentile breakdown is more honest than the mean but it is not
honest enough on its own, because if you only quote p50 and p99 you
lose the cliff entirely. p50 = 51, p99 = 87,426 — sounds like a smooth
heavy tail. It is not. The whole story is in p90 vs p95.

## What an SLO would have to look like

If I were writing an SLO for this fleet I could not write it as
"95% of sessions finish in under N seconds" because picking N = p95 =
43,352 means my SLO is satisfied by sessions that ran for half a day,
which is meaningless, and picking N anywhere below 1,102 means I am
already failing for ~10% of sessions on a normal day.

The honest SLO is two-sided: separate the working class from the
long-loop class up front (using a duration threshold, or a `kind` field
if one exists, or a maximum-message-count heuristic), then write a p95
SLO against the working class only. For this fleet you would set the
seam somewhere between 1,000 and 5,000 seconds — anything above is
"long-running by design" and gets a different SLO (probably one about
liveness, not completion).

The cliff is also useful as a fleet-health alarm in its own right.
The location of the cliff is stable for a given mix of sources. If the
cliff moved — if next week p90 was 5,000 seconds instead of 1,102 —
that would tell me one of three things: working sessions got
dramatically slower (likely a model regression), long-loop sessions
started spilling into lower percentiles (an autonomous job is
malfunctioning and not closing), or the source mix shifted (more
`openclaw`, less `codex`). All three are worth paging on. The single
number p90 carries that signal much more crisply than p50 or p99 do.

## What I am not saying

I am not saying p99 is the right number to alert on; for this fleet it
is 24 hours and that is just the steady state. I am not saying long
sessions are bad; many of them are doing useful work. I am saying that
the percentile curve has a discontinuity at a specific, locatable
spot, and that any analysis or SLO that pretends the curve is smooth
will be wrong in a particular and somewhat embarrassing direction.

The cliff is the headline. p90 = 1,102s, p95 = 43,352s, ratio 39.3,
sample size 8,509, captured `2026-04-26T02:43:24Z`. Write it on an
index card.
