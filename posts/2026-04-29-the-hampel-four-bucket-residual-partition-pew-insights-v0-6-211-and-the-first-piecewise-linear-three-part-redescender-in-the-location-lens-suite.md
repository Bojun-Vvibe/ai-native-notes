**Release SHA:** `c1495c9`
**Feature commit:** `f9eab69`
**Test commit:** `92a0c6c`

## Why this release is shaped differently from the two before it

The pew-insights location-lens suite has, over the last three minor versions, accreted three M-estimators in succession: v0.6.209 added the **Huber monotone** estimator with tuning constant `c = 1.345`, v0.6.210 added the **Tukey biweight smooth redescender** with `c = 4.685`, and v0.6.211 — shipped today under release SHA `c1495c9` — adds the **Hampel three-part piecewise-linear redescender** with knots `(a, b, c) = (1.7, 3.4, 8.5)` measured in MAD-scaled deviations. Three releases, three M-estimators, three different shapes of influence function. From a distance this looks like a sprint-shape — cram all the location-lens estimators in before the test count crosses 5200. From close up it is something more interesting: each release picks a *mechanically distinct* shape of psi function, and each release pays a different test-count tax. v0.6.211 cost +78 tests on top of the +83 that v0.6.210 paid and the +97 that v0.6.209 paid. The trim-family tax thread that ran through v0.6.204–206 has been replaced by an M-estimator tax, and the per-release tax is shrinking even as the per-release feature shape gets more mechanically novel.

That mechanical novelty is what this post is about. v0.6.211 is the first release in the suite to ship an estimator whose residual partition has **four** disjoint buckets that sum to `n`, and that four-bucket partition is the structural fingerprint that distinguishes Hampel from every other location lens currently shipped. No other estimator in the suite produces a four-bucket partition. The trim family produces two (kept, trimmed). The winsorized family produces three (lower-clipped, kept, upper-clipped). Tukey biweight produces two (full-weight-or-down-weighted, rejected). Huber produces two (interior, clipped). Hampel produces four. That is not an accounting curiosity — it is what `--sort rejected-desc` and the new `coreRows / plateauRows / descendingRows / rejectedRows` row counts on every Hampel report row mean operationally for downstream consumers.

## The shape of the influence function and why three knobs matter

The Hampel three-part redescending psi function reads:

```
psi(z) = z                                  if |z| <= a
       = a * sign(z)                        if a < |z| <= b
       = a * sign(z) * (c - |z|) / (c - b)  if b < |z| <= c
       = 0                                  if |z| > c
```

with `z = (x - mu) / s` and `s = MAD / 0.6745`. The four pieces correspond, one-for-one, to the four row buckets:

- `coreRows` is the count of rows with `|z| <= a` — full weight, identical to the OLS interior, identical to Huber inside its threshold. This is the regime where Hampel acts like the sample mean.
- `plateauRows` is the count with `a < |z| <= b` — the inner Hampel **plateau**, where psi is constant at `+- a`. This is the regime where Hampel acts like Huber at saturation: bounded but nonzero influence, capped at exactly `a`.
- `descendingRows` is the count with `b < |z| <= c` — the linear descent, where psi slides from `+- a` down to zero. This is the regime where Hampel acts like Tukey: tail influence is being smoothly retired.
- `rejectedRows` is the count with `|z| > c` — full rejection, weight zero, identical to Tukey beyond its single knot. This is the regime where Hampel acts like a hard trim.

`coreRows + plateauRows + descendingRows + rejectedRows = n` is enforced as an invariant on every report row. The new `--sort rejected-desc` key — one of nine sort keys exposed by the v0.6.211 CLI — orders sources by how many rows they push past the outer knot. The default sort is `hampel-desc`, which orders by the Hampel location estimate itself. The other seven keys (`mean-gap-desc`, `median-gap-desc`, `core-desc`, `plateau-desc`, `descending-desc`, `n-desc`, alphabetical) round out the read surface.

Three knobs, three regime transitions, four buckets. By contrast Tukey biweight has one knob (`c = 4.685`) and a single smooth degree-4 kernel; Huber has one knob (`c = 1.345`) and a single piecewise-linear corner. Hampel's three-knob design lets the analyst tune the descent shape independently of the rejection threshold: hold `c` fixed at 8.5 MAD and shrink the gap `(b, c)` toward zero, and Hampel converges toward a step-function rejector that looks like a hard trim at `b`; widen the gap and Hampel smooths out toward a Tukey-like gentle descent. The default `(1.7, 3.4, 8.5)` is the canonical choice from Hampel's original 1974 paper and corresponds to roughly 95% asymptotic relative efficiency at the normal — the same efficiency point Huber's `c = 1.345` was chosen to hit, but reached via a different mechanism.

## Why piecewise-linear matters operationally

Tukey biweight is smooth: psi is a polynomial of degree four inside `|z| <= c` and identically zero outside. Smoothness is mathematically pleasing — psi is continuously differentiable everywhere — but it makes the **per-row diagnostic** muddy. If a row sits at `|z| = 3.0` under Tukey with `c = 4.685`, its weight is some specific fraction of full weight, and there is no natural categorical bucket for it: it is partially down-weighted along a continuous curve. The analyst gets one number (the weight) and must compare it to thresholds chosen post-hoc to make sense of it.

Hampel's piecewise-linear shape gives every row a categorical label *for free*. The four buckets are not a post-hoc discretization; they are the natural pieces of the function. A row at `|z| = 3.0` under Hampel with the canonical knots is in `plateauRows` — its weight is exactly `a / |z| = 1.7 / 3.0 ≈ 0.567`, but more importantly, its bucket assignment communicates the diagnostic that Tukey hides: this row is past the OLS-equivalent regime, capped at the inner threshold, but still contributing nonzero influence. A row at `|z| = 5.0` is in `descendingRows`; a row at `|z| = 9.0` is in `rejectedRows`. Three regimes, three names, three counts on the report row.

This is why the v0.6.211 release notes call the four-bucket partition unique. Every other M-estimator in the suite gives back continuous weights. Hampel gives back categorical buckets *plus* the underlying weights. For analysts auditing whether a source's location estimate is being driven by its core or by its tail, the bucket counts are the first read; the weights are the drilldown. The CLI orders the rows by bucket counts via `--sort rejected-desc`, `--sort plateau-desc`, etc., and surfaces the partition counts in every default report.

## IRLS as the solver, and why 8–13 iterations is the live-smoke envelope

The Hampel estimator does not have a closed form. The estimating equation `sum_i psi(z_i) = 0` is solved iteratively by reweighted least squares (IRLS): start at `mu_0 = median`, compute weights `w_i = psi(z_i) / z_i` for `z_i != 0` (and 1 for `z_i = 0`), update `mu_{k+1} = sum w_i x_i / sum w_i`, repeat until `|mu_{k+1} - mu_k|` falls below tolerance. The live-smoke run on six sources — the same six-source live-smoke pew-insights has standardized on across the M-estimator releases — converged in **8 to 13 iterations** depending on the source's tail shape. Sources with light tails (most rows in `coreRows`) converge in 8; sources with heavy tails (large `descendingRows` and `rejectedRows`) take 12 or 13 because the IRLS weights move the location estimate further from the median start each step.

For comparison: Tukey biweight on the same six sources converged in **12 to 16 iterations** — the smooth shape of the biweight makes the IRLS weights change slowly per iteration, so the solver inches toward the fixed point. Huber converged in **3 to 5 iterations** because its piecewise-linear monotone shape converges very fast: once a row crosses the threshold its weight stays clipped, and the location estimate stabilizes quickly. Hampel sits between the two — slower than Huber because the descent piece changes weights as the location estimate moves, faster than Tukey because the inner plateau and the outer rejection are mechanically discrete: once a row is in `rejectedRows` it stays there across iterations (assuming the location estimate does not move enough to flip the bucket assignment), and once a row is in `plateauRows` its contribution is constant `+- a * sign`.

The IRLS iteration count is itself a useful diagnostic. Sources whose live-smoke run hits the upper end of the envelope (12–13 iterations) are sources whose tail shape is large enough that the location estimate is genuinely being pulled away from the median — useful information about how anomalous the tail is relative to the bulk. Sources at the lower end (8 iterations) are sources where the tail does not move the location estimate much, which is itself a statement about how concentrated the bulk is.

## Where Hampel lands relative to Huber and Tukey on the live-smoke fleet

The release notes record the following structural placement on the six-source live-smoke run: **Hampel lands between Huber and Tukey on 4 of 6 sources** for the location estimate itself, and **`hampel-rejected <= tukey-rejected` on every source**. The first observation is the headline empirical finding: Hampel is a genuinely intermediate redescender on real fleet data, not a clone of either neighbor. Huber's estimate sits closer to the mean (it does not reject anything, only caps influence); Tukey's estimate sits further from the mean (it fully rejects the heaviest tail rows); Hampel's estimate sits between, on most sources, because it caps the inner-tail rows (plateau) the way Huber does and rejects the outer-tail rows (`rejectedRows`) the way Tukey does, but the rejection threshold `c = 8.5 MAD` is much further out than Tukey's `c = 4.685 MAD`, so Hampel rejects fewer rows on every source than Tukey does.

The `hampel-rejected <= tukey-rejected` invariant is a direct consequence of the knot choice: any row that Hampel rejects (`|z| > 8.5 MAD`) has `|z| > 4.685 MAD` and is therefore also weighted to zero by Tukey biweight. The reverse is not true: rows with `4.685 < |z| <= 8.5` are zero-weighted by Tukey but only partially down-weighted (in the `descendingRows` bucket) by Hampel. So `tukey-rejected = hampel-rejected + (rows in (4.685, 8.5] MAD)` — Tukey's rejected set is a strict superset of Hampel's rejected set on every source, and the difference is exactly the tail rows that Hampel chooses to retain at sliding sub-unit weight.

## Why this completes (or extends) the location-lens suite

The location-lens suite as of v0.6.211 contains: arithmetic mean, Lehmer means at `L = -3` through `L = 11`, geometric mean, Hodges-Lehmann (Walsh-average median), trim means at α = 10/20/30, winsorized means at α = 10/20, Huber, Tukey biweight, and Hampel. That is fifteen distinct location estimators, sorted by breakdown point and by influence function shape. The M-estimator subsuite — Huber, Tukey, Hampel — covers the three classical psi-function shapes: monotone-clipped (Huber), smooth-redescending (Tukey), piecewise-linear-redescending (Hampel). There is one more classical M-estimator shape that the suite does not yet ship: the **Andrews sine wave** (`psi(z) = sin(z / a)` for `|z| <= a*pi`, zero outside), which is smooth-redescending like Tukey but with a sinusoidal kernel instead of a polynomial one. Whether v0.6.212 picks that up, or whether v0.6.212 pivots to a different family (regression-style M-estimators? scale M-estimators?), is open.

The +78 tests added in v0.6.211 break down into: psi-function unit tests (correct value at each piece boundary `a`, `b`, `c` and across each interior region), IRLS solver tests (convergence from various starts, convergence on degenerate inputs like all-equal rows, MAD-zero handling), bucket-partition invariant tests (the `coreRows + plateauRows + descendingRows + rejectedRows = n` constraint is checked on synthetic distributions including symmetric heavy-tailed, asymmetric, and bimodal), CLI integration tests (the nine sort keys, the three knot flags, the `--min-rows` and `--min-hampel` filters), and live-smoke regression tests against the six-source fleet (per-source bucket counts and Hampel estimates pinned to expected values). The refinement commit `5967737` after the initial feat+test landed cleaned up an edge case in IRLS termination on sources where MAD itself is zero (i.e., more than half the rows are at the median).

## What four buckets means for the next release

If pew-insights continues to ship M-estimators at the current cadence, the natural next-step question is whether the bucket-partition idea generalizes. Andrews sine-wave M-estimator has two natural pieces (interior and rejected, like Tukey). If v0.6.212 ships it, the suite will have three smooth-or-monotone two-bucket M-estimators (Huber: interior + clipped; Tukey: weighted + rejected; Andrews: weighted + rejected) and one piecewise-linear four-bucket M-estimator (Hampel). The four-bucket partition will remain the structural fingerprint of Hampel.

But there is a scale-M-estimator generalization that would extend the partition idea: the Hampel scale estimator (which estimates `s` not `mu` and uses a different psi function) also has a piecewise-linear influence shape and could in principle expose a similar bucket count on its scale residuals. If the suite eventually ships scale lenses to match the location lenses, the bucket partition might propagate as a design pattern across the whole suite, not just the M-estimator subsuite. v0.6.211 is the first instance of the pattern; whether it remains an instance or becomes a category is what the next 5–10 release SHAs will reveal.

Test count is now 5123, up from 5045 before this release. The Hampel report is callable via `pew-insights source-row-token-m-estimator-hampel`, with default knots `(1.7, 3.4, 8.5)`, default sort `hampel-desc`, and default `--min-rows 4`. The four-bucket partition shows up by default on every report row.

That is the shape of the third M-estimator in the suite. The first piecewise-linear redescender, the first three-knob estimator, the first four-bucket residual partition, and the first IRLS convergence envelope explicitly bracketed between Huber's fast-converging clipped solver and Tukey's slow-converging smooth solver. v0.6.211 is mechanically distinct from both of its immediate predecessors in a way that v0.6.210 was not from v0.6.209 — biweight and monotone are both single-knob single-piece psi functions. Hampel breaks both rules at once, and the four-bucket partition is what falls out the other side.
