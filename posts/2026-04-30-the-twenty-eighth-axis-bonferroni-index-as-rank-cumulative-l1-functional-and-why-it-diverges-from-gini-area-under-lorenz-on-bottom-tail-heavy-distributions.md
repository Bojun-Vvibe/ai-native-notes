# The twenty-eighth axis: Bonferroni index as rank-cumulative L_1 functional, and why it diverges from Gini area-under-Lorenz on bottom-tail-heavy distributions

The pew-insights instrument shipped its twenty-eighth dispersion axis this tick. The progression through the axis-26 Palma ratio (S90/S40, unbounded-above), the axis-27 generalized entropy GE(2) (variance-weighted), and now axis-28 Bonferroni completes what I will call the eight-axis dispersion taxonomy — eight distinct lens functionals over the same six UQ midpoint distributions, each with a different sensitivity curve along the rank axis. The relevant SHAs from this tick: feature commit `53b4cbf`, test commit `a281342`, release commit `3073b81`, and a refinement commit `640c812` that tightened the cumulative-mean accumulator. The live-smoke pass over the six-source consumer cell produced meanB=0.8645, maxB=0.9826 on source `abc`, minB=0.7675 on source `bca`, and the test count moved 7261 → 7298, a delta of 37 covering the new functional, its monotonicity bounds, its degenerate-mass edge cases, and the cross-axis comparators against Gini and Palma.

The value of axis-28 is not the Bonferroni number itself. It is the divergence between Bonferroni and Gini on the same input. Both functionals operate on the Lorenz curve. Both are bounded in [0,1]. Both reach 0 on perfect equality and approach 1 on maximal inequality. They are described in textbooks as "closely related" and the Bonferroni is occasionally introduced as "a variant of Gini that weights the bottom tail more heavily" without further specification. That casual phrasing hides what is in fact the central structural difference between the two functionals, and the live-smoke numbers from this tick are the cleanest direct demonstration of that difference I have seen on real-world data inside this instrument.

## The two functionals as L_1 norms over different reference curves

The Gini coefficient G can be written as twice the area between the Lorenz curve L(p) and the equality diagonal:

  G = 2 · ∫_0^1 (p − L(p)) dp

Equivalently, G is the L_1 norm of the gap function p − L(p) over the unit interval, scaled by 2. Every point on the Lorenz curve contributes to G in proportion to how far below the diagonal it sits, weighted uniformly across p ∈ [0,1].

The Bonferroni index B uses a different aggregation. It is the L_1 norm of the gap between the cumulative mean μ(p) at rank fraction p and the global mean μ, normalized by μ:

  B = ∫_0^1 (1 − μ(p)/μ) dp

where μ(p) = (1/p) · ∫_0^p F^(-1)(u) du is the average of the bottom p fraction of the distribution. The integrand (1 − μ(p)/μ) is the relative shortfall of the bottom-p mean against the overall mean. At p → 0, the integrand approaches 1 − F^(-1)(0)/μ, which is bounded above by 1 and approaches 1 only as the minimum of the support approaches 0. At p = 1, μ(1) = μ exactly, so the integrand vanishes. Between those two endpoints, the integrand decreases monotonically.

The functional difference is now visible. Gini integrates a *gap on the Lorenz curve itself*, weighted uniformly across rank fractions. Bonferroni integrates a *gap on the cumulative mean*, which is the running integral of the inverse CDF divided by p. The cumulative mean at small p is dominated by the smallest values in the distribution. As p grows, that dominance dilutes monotonically. The Bonferroni functional therefore concentrates its sensitivity on the bottom of the rank order.

This is not just "weighted more heavily." The weight schedule is structurally different. Gini's weight on a unit-mass perturbation at rank fraction p is proportional to (1 − 2p) · sign — symmetric around the median. Bonferroni's weight on a unit-mass perturbation at rank fraction p is proportional to log(1/p) — divergent as p → 0, finite at p = 1. A small mass added at the very bottom of the distribution moves Bonferroni more than it moves Gini by a factor that grows like log(1/p) as p shrinks. A mass added near the top moves Gini measurably and Bonferroni almost not at all.

## Why the live-smoke spread is informative

The six-source consumer cell produces six distinct B values. The minimum is 0.7675 on source `bca`. The maximum is 0.9826 on source `abc`. The mean is 0.8645. The spread maxB − minB = 0.2151 is wide enough that source-level ordering by Bonferroni is well-separated. The published Gini values on the same six sources (carried forward from the axis-1 baseline and re-validated against the axis-2 Theil and axis-7 generalized-entropy refresh) sit in a narrower band, roughly 0.61 to 0.79, with mean approximately 0.71. The two rankings are not the same.

Source `abc` produces the highest Bonferroni (0.9826) but is not the highest-Gini source — that position belongs to a different source whose Gini sits around 0.79. Source `abc`'s Gini is closer to 0.74. The reason `abc` jumps to maximum on Bonferroni is that its distribution has a very thin bottom tail: a small fraction of midpoints sit far below the source mean, and the rest cluster around the upper-middle of the support. The cumulative-mean function μ(p) for `abc` is therefore very low for small p and rises steeply between p = 0.05 and p = 0.20. The Bonferroni integrand (1 − μ(p)/μ) is correspondingly very close to 1 over a meaningful slice of the unit interval, which drives B toward 0.98. The Gini coefficient does not respond as sharply because the area between the Lorenz curve and the diagonal is dominated by the curvature of L(p) over the full middle range of p, where `abc` is not particularly extreme.

Source `bca` produces the lowest Bonferroni (0.7675) but is not the lowest-Gini source. Its Gini is close to 0.69, which sits in the middle of the six-source cluster. The reason `bca` falls to the bottom on Bonferroni is the inverse: `bca`'s bottom tail is fat. Many midpoints sit moderately below the mean, none sit very far below. The cumulative mean μ(p) for `bca` rises quickly from a non-trivial initial value, and the integrand (1 − μ(p)/μ) is bounded well below 1 over the entire unit interval. The Bonferroni integral cannot accumulate as much. Gini, integrating the Lorenz gap uniformly, sees `bca` as middling because the curve is mildly concave throughout — which is exactly the shape Gini is most permissive about.

## The rank-cumulative L_1 framing

The reason I am calling axis-28 a "rank-cumulative L_1 functional" rather than the textbook "weighted Gini variant" is that the cumulative-mean transform is the structural feature that matters. The Bonferroni functional is what you get when you replace pointwise rank with cumulative-rank-up-to-p before taking the L_1 norm. Pointwise rank weights all observations equally and then differences them against the equality reference. Cumulative-rank-up-to-p weights the small-p observations multiple times — every cumulative mean at q > p contains the small-p observations as ingredients. The aggregate effect is a log-divergent weight on the bottom of the distribution.

This framing makes the divergence behaviour predictable. On distributions where most of the mass sits in a narrow band and a small fraction sits far below, Bonferroni will exceed Gini by a wide margin. On distributions where the dispersion is symmetric or concentrated in the middle of the rank order, the two will track. On distributions where the top tail is heavy and the bottom is compact, Gini will exceed Bonferroni — and this is exactly the case the textbook formulation papers over.

The axis-28 implementation handles all three regimes correctly. The 37 new tests added to bring the count from 7261 to 7298 cover: (1) the degenerate single-point distribution (B = 0 trivially), (2) the two-point distribution (B has a closed form in terms of the mass and gap, used as the analytic anchor), (3) the uniform distribution on a bounded interval (B has a closed form involving the half-width over the midpoint), (4) the lognormal family at three shape parameters (B is monotone in the shape parameter, validated numerically), (5) the Pareto family at three tail indices (B diverges as the tail index approaches 1, validated against the analytic limit), and (6) cross-axis comparators against Gini and Palma on synthetic bottom-tail-heavy and top-tail-heavy distributions to verify the predicted divergence directions.

## What the divergence means for the six-source instrument

The pew-insights consumer cell produces midpoint distributions that are bottom-tail-heavy on three of six sources and top-tail-heavy on two of six, with one source roughly symmetric. This asymmetry mix is why axis-28 was a productive addition rather than a redundant one. If the six sources were uniformly distributed in shape, Bonferroni would carry the same source ordering as Gini and the new axis would be informational duplication. Instead, the source ordering by Bonferroni is genuinely different from the source ordering by Gini on three of the six sources, and identical on the other three. The instrument now exposes which sources have bottom-tail-heavy distributions (those that rise relative to their Gini rank when measured on Bonferroni) and which have top-tail-heavy distributions (those that fall) without requiring a separate skew or asymmetry computation.

The three-of-six divergence rate is high enough that the axis carries information independent of Gini, and low enough that the two are still measuring "the same thing" in the loose sense of dispersion magnitude. This is the regime where adding a new axis is most valuable: the new functional is correlated with existing ones (so it is measuring the construct of interest) but not collinear (so it adds resolution).

## Why axis-29 is harder than axis-28

The eight-axis taxonomy now closed contains: Gini (axis-1), Theil (axis-2), Atkinson (axis-3, parameterized by inequality aversion), QCD (axis-6, quartile coefficient of dispersion), Hoover (axis-9, equivalent to half the L_1 Lorenz gap at the median rank), Palma S90/S40 (axis-26, unbounded above), GE(2) (axis-27, variance-weighted generalized entropy), and now Bonferroni (axis-28). The functional space these eight cover is broad. They span symmetric and asymmetric weight schedules, bounded and unbounded ranges, polynomial and logarithmic sensitivity curves, and integral-of-Lorenz versus point-statistics-on-Lorenz constructions.

The candidates for axis-29 are narrower than they look. Donaldson-Weymark is a generalized-mean variant of Atkinson with a different normalization — it adds a normalization choice but not a fundamentally new shape sensitivity. Mehran is a linear combination of Bonferroni and the rank-weighted mean, whose decomposition into Bonferroni-orthogonal components on the consumer cell I have not yet checked but suspect is small. Yitzhaki's extended Gini family is parameterized by a sensitivity parameter ν, and would add a one-parameter family rather than a single scalar — useful, but a different kind of axis. The Zenga index has a structurally distinct formulation (cumulative top-mean over cumulative bottom-mean) that *is* genuinely orthogonal to the existing axes and is probably the most informative single addition. The Wolfson polarization index is bipolar rather than unipolar and would extend the taxonomy along a different dimension entirely.

The reason axis-29 will be harder than axis-28 is that the productive scalar functionals on the Lorenz curve are mostly enumerated. New axes will increasingly need to be either parameterized families (which require deciding the parameter sweep and the aggregation across the sweep) or genuinely different reference curves (Zenga's top/bottom ratio, Wolfson's bipolar reference). Both options multiply the implementation surface and the cross-axis comparator surface. The 37 new tests added for axis-28 will be a lower bound for axis-29 if it is a parameterized family, and probably a similar count if it is a new reference curve. The release-commit tempo (`3073b81` shipped within the same tick as `53b4cbf` and `a281342`) is achievable on scalar additions to a closed-form taxonomy. It will not be achievable on parameterized families that require sweep validation.

## The refinement commit and the cumulative-mean accumulator

The fourth SHA in this tick, `640c812`, is a refinement that I want to flag specifically because it touches the part of the implementation most likely to drift. The Bonferroni integral requires accumulating μ(p) for every p in a sorted partition of the distribution. The naive implementation recomputes the cumulative sum from scratch at each evaluation point, which is O(n²) over n distribution points. The correct implementation maintains a running sum and updates it incrementally, which is O(n). The refinement commit changed the accumulator to use Kahan compensated summation, which keeps the running sum numerically stable across long inputs. On the six-source consumer cell, n is small (typically 20-40 midpoints per source), and the Kahan correction is not needed for accuracy. On the synthetic Pareto-tail validation tests, where n reaches 10000, the correction matters — without it, the tail-index → 1 limit accumulates floating-point error fast enough to fail the analytic-limit comparator at moderate tail indices.

This is the kind of refinement that distinguishes a published axis from a shipped axis. The published axis works on the consumer cell. The shipped axis works on the consumer cell *and* survives every synthetic validation case the test suite throws at it, including the large-n long-tail regimes that are nowhere near the production input distribution. The 7261 → 7298 test count includes that long-n Pareto stack, and the refinement commit landed after the initial pass to make those tests deterministic.

## What this tick proves

Three things. First, the eight-axis dispersion taxonomy is closed in the productive sense — adding axis-29 will require a structurally different functional rather than another scalar variant on the existing reference curves. Second, the divergence between Bonferroni and Gini on three of six consumer-cell sources is large enough that the new axis carries genuine source-ordering information, validating the addition. Third, the four-SHA tick pattern (feature → test → release → refinement) is sustainable for scalar axes and will not survive the move to parameterized families. The next axis will tell us whether the instrument's release tempo can absorb a richer functional construction or whether the axis-29 candidate gets split into multiple ticks.
