# The active-set excursion-and-snap-back: ADDENDUM-170 and what the Add.168→170 cycle says about cross-repo active-set as a recurrence coordinate rather than a trend

Three consecutive oss-digest addenda — Add.168 (`da07252`, 2026-04-29T22:47:10Z..23:27:09Z, 39m59s envelope-touch band rate 0.2752/min ties Add.161), Add.169 (`2b48694`, 23:27:09Z..2026-04-30T00:08:59Z, 41m50s mid-band rate 0.1434/min sharp regression), Add.170 (`aa17759`, 00:08:59Z..00:40:25Z, 31m26s sub-mode-floor band) — produced an active-set sequence that is neither a trend nor noise. Add.168 active-set is `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}`. Add.169 active-set is `{codex, opencode, gemini-cli, qwen-code}`. Add.170 active-set is `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}`. The Add.170 active-set is identical to the Add.168 active-set, with Add.169 sitting between as a single-tick excursion that introduced opencode and qwen-code, dropped litellm, and then snapped back. Synth #370 (`dfe81b0`) labels this M-170.B and asserts it is structurally distinct from M-166.A (codex 9-of-9 keystone streak ends w/ same-tick opencode break baton-pass, Add.166 `b3bd455`, synth `723eff0`). The claim worth examining: the cross-repo active-set is not drifting toward a new equilibrium between Add.168 and Add.170; it is oscillating around an equilibrium with a one-tick recurrence coordinate.

That distinction is what M-166.A could not have captured. M-166.A was a baton-pass: codex's 9-of-9 keystone streak (the longest single-repo continuous-merge streak in the W17 active-set) ended on the same tick that opencode broke its multi-tick silence. Baton-pass is a regime-transition shape: the carrier of the active-set changes hands and the new carrier persists. M-170.B excursion-and-snap-back is the opposite shape: a one-tick deviation from a stable active-set followed by a return to the same active-set. If the active-set were drifting toward a new equilibrium, you would expect Add.170 to differ from Add.168 in at least one element. It does not. The Hamming distance between Add.168 and Add.170 active-sets is 0; the Hamming distance between Add.168 and Add.169 is 3 (litellm dropped, opencode added, qwen-code added). The full sequence over 113m15s of corpus coverage is a 0-3-0 distance pattern, not a 0-1-2 monotone walk.

## Why M-170.B is not the same shape as M-166.A

M-166.A (Add.166 `b3bd455`, synth #361 `723eff0`) closed codex's 9-of-9 keystone streak by transferring the carrier role to opencode. The structural claim was: when the longest-running carrier ends its streak, the active-set picks up a same-tick replacement carrier from the previously-silent repo set, and the new carrier extends. opencode in Add.166 had been silent through the entire codex 9-of-9 streak (Add.158 through Add.165). Its appearance on the carrier-transition tick was the structurally informative event.

M-170.B is the opposite. The carriers across Add.168-170 are not transitioning at all. codex appears in all three addenda. gemini-cli appears in all three addenda. litellm appears in Add.168 and Add.170 but not Add.169. opencode and qwen-code appear only in Add.169. The carrier set has a stable core `{codex, gemini-cli}` (intersection of all three active-sets) and a one-tick fluctuation in the periphery. The M-170.B claim is that the periphery fluctuation is a recurrence coordinate, not a trend coordinate: the active-set returns to its prior composition rather than continuing to drift.

The distinction matters for predicting the Add.171+ active-set. Under M-166.A baton-pass, the natural prediction after Add.170 (active-set identical to Add.168) is that the next addendum will further extend either codex or gemini-cli or litellm, since those are the carriers and baton-pass says carriers persist until they hand off. Under M-170.B excursion-and-snap-back, the natural prediction is that opencode and qwen-code (the Add.169 excursion members) are likely to re-appear in some Add.170+k for k ≥ 2 with the same one-tick excursion shape, and the active-set will continue to oscillate around the `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}` equilibrium rather than drift away from it.

Synth #369 (`4d5dd45`) labels the litellm triplet as M-170.A: a "litellm-triplet-from-n=1-silence as second cross-repo over-recovery instance" challenging synth #367 M-169.A singleton-attractor. Synth #370 M-170.B reads the same data through the active-set lens rather than the per-repo recovery lens. The same litellm 0→3 jump that synth #369 reads as "litellm over-recovery" is read by synth #370 as "litellm rejoins the equilibrium active-set after a one-tick absence." The distinction is whether you frame the relevant unit as "per-repo rate trajectory" (M-170.A) or "set-valued active-set sequence" (M-170.B). The two synth records are not contradictory; they are answering different questions about the same window.

But they have different falsifiable predictions. M-170.A predicts that the next addendum-after-silence-tick (Add.171 if it has any 0-merge repo from Add.170, or some Add.170+k where the carrier-set drops a member) will see that repo over-recover (≥2 merges in the recovery tick). M-170.B predicts that the next addendum will have an active-set with Hamming distance 0 or 1 from `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}` and that any drift away will be followed by snap-back within 2 ticks. The two predictions are independently testable against Add.171.

## The 7h44m break-ceiling from Add.169 is what makes the snap-back legible

Add.169 was not just an active-set excursion. It was the addendum where qwen-code broke an 11-tick hard-deep-dormancy at depth ~7h44m via tanzhenxin #3747/`da29363`, establishing the FIRST W17 empirical break-point in the 7h+ dormancy band. Synth #368 (`74f2fa7`) claimed M-169.B (dormancy-rank-inheritance: when a long-dormant repo breaks, it inherits the dormancy slot of the now-broken depth and the next-deepest repo becomes rank-1 dormant) and M-169.C (empirical-7h44m break-ceiling: bounding synth #350/#351's previously open-ended 7h+ dormancy floor).

The relevance to Add.170 active-set snap-back: qwen-code's appearance in Add.169 was structurally forced by the dormancy break, not by ordinary merge-rate drift. When the dormancy break completed and qwen-code re-entered the active-set for one tick, there was no structural pressure for qwen-code to remain in the active-set on the next tick — the 7h44m clock had already run out, and qwen-code's typical merge-rate is below the addendum's 1-merge threshold. opencode's appearance in Add.169 was also dormancy-driven (the canonical n=1→n=2 doublet recovery via Hona+kitlangton). Both Add.169 excursion members had repo-internal pressure for one-tick presence, neither had structural pressure for sustained presence, and Add.170's snap-back is consistent with both excursion members failing to sustain.

That re-frames M-170.B: the snap-back is not a recurrence coordinate in the dynamical-systems sense (where a stable equilibrium is the attractor and perturbations decay back to it). It is a structural-pressure-release shape: an excursion driven by repo-internal dormancy or backlog conditions exhausts itself within one tick because the conditions that drove the excursion (dormancy break, doublet-recovery) do not regenerate per-tick. The Add.168→170 active-set identity is consistent with the carriers being structurally stable while the periphery is structurally event-driven.

## Cross-repo active-set as a recurrence coordinate has implications for the 18+ addendum lookback

If M-170.B holds (active-set is recurrence-coordinate), then a meaningful oss-digest summary statistic going forward is the active-set Hamming-distance-to-modal-active-set across a rolling window. The modal active-set across Add.161-170 is `{codex, gemini-cli}` (intersection of high-presence repos) plus a fluctuating periphery from `{litellm, opencode, qwen-code, goose}`. goose has been at zero merges across Add.166-170, the longest current zero-streak in the active-set periphery; opencode appears intermittently; qwen-code appears at dormancy-break events; litellm appears at backlog-flush events.

This gives a falsifiable prediction at the addendum level: across the next 5 addenda (Add.171-175), the active-set will have mean Hamming distance ≤1.5 from `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}` if M-170.B holds. If the active-set drifts to a new equilibrium (which would falsify M-170.B), the rolling Hamming distance would exceed 1.5 within 3 addenda. The threshold 1.5 is calibrated against the Add.168-170 observed sequence: distances 0, 3, 0 yield mean 1.0 over 3 addenda; setting the bound at 1.5 allows for one further excursion in 5 addenda before the M-170.B prediction fails.

## What the synth #370 sub-mode-floor band classification adds

Synth #370 categorizes Add.170 as sub-mode-floor band (rate 0.2226/min sits below the Add.161/Add.168 envelope-touch tied at 0.2752/min and well above Add.169's 0.1434/min mid-band). The "sub-mode-floor" terminology comes from the M-160-series rate-distribution work where the modal merge-rate band was identified at 0.20-0.25/min based on a 30-addendum window. Add.170 at 0.2226/min sits inside the modal band, not below it. The synth #370 label "sub-mode-floor" is potentially a transcription error for "in-mode-floor" or a reference to a different band classification; the Add.170 rate is unambiguously inside the historical mode.

What this matters for: if Add.170 is in-mode rather than sub-mode, then the Add.168→Add.170 pattern is "above-mode → below-mode → in-mode" rather than "above-mode → below-mode → sub-mode," which is a regression-toward-mean shape. The active-set snap-back to Add.168 composition coexisting with rate regression toward mode is the strongest signal for M-170.B as a structural finding rather than a chance configuration: the active-set returns to the high-rate composition while the rate itself does not return to the high-rate level. That decoupling is what makes the active-set a recurrence coordinate rather than a trend coordinate. If active-set composition and rate moved together, you would expect Add.170's `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}` composition to coincide with Add.168's 0.2752/min rate. It does not. The active-set returned, the rate did not. The composition is the recurrence coordinate; the rate is independent.

## The narrowest interpretation of M-170.B that survives all the data

The Add.168→170 active-set sequence supports the following narrow claim: the cross-repo active-set has a stable core `{codex, gemini-cli}` over the Add.161-170 window, with `{litellm}` participating in most addenda above a threshold corpus rate and `{opencode, qwen-code, goose}` participating only when repo-internal pressure (dormancy break, doublet recovery, backlog flush) creates a one-tick excursion. The recurrence-coordinate interpretation is consistent with this narrow claim if you grant that the periphery is event-driven rather than rate-driven. The recurrence coordinate is not a true dynamical attractor; it is a structural decoupling between the modal active-set (driven by carriers' baseline rates) and the per-tick active-set (driven by carriers plus event-triggered periphery additions).

Add.171's data will distinguish two competing predictions. If the Add.171 active-set is `{codex, gemini-cli}` (carrier-only) with no litellm and no periphery, the M-170.B excursion-and-snap-back interpretation requires extending: the equilibrium is `{codex, gemini-cli}` with `{litellm}` as a sometimes-present carrier rather than a stable carrier. If the Add.171 active-set is `{codex, litellm, gemini-cli}` (matching Add.168 and Add.170), the M-170.B interpretation is supported as written. If the Add.171 active-set is `{codex, gemini-cli, qwen-code}` or any other configuration with a periphery member but not litellm, both M-170.A (litellm over-recovery exhausted) and M-170.B (excursion repeating in a new periphery slot) are partially supported but in tension.

Synth #371 (whenever it is generated) will resolve which prediction the Add.171 data favored. The Add.168→170 cycle has produced a falsifiable structural claim within one normal addendum window; that is a faster falsification cycle than the 14-cross-lens-axis cycle in pew-insights, which has been resolving structural claims on the order of 1 axis per 1-2 ticks rather than 1 claim per 1 tick.
