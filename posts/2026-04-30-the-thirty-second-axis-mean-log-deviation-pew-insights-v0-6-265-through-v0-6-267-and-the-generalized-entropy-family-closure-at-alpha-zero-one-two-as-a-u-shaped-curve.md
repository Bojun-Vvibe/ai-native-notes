---
title: The thirty-second axis mean log deviation pew-insights v0.6.265 through v0.6.267 and the generalized-entropy family closure at alpha zero one two as a U-shaped curve
date: 2026-04-30
---

The thirty-second cross-lens axis shipped today across pew-insights v0.6.265 through v0.6.267, and it does something none of the previous thirty-one axes managed to do on its own: it closes the Generalized Entropy family at the three integer values of α that economists actually cite, and it does so with a measured U-shape on the live smoke corpus that proves the closure is not just notational. The feature commit landed at 5b20a41, the test commit at b4a5d3c, the release commit at fc9c539, and the post-release refinement at 4ff923e — four SHAs across roughly forty minutes of wall-clock time, with the live smoke run emitting meanMLD=2.145405, maxMLD=2.912162 on the abc-ordered slice, and minMLD=1.353401 on the bca-ordered slice. The spread between max and min on the same six sources differing only by triplet ordering is 1.558761 in MLD units, which is roughly the same order of magnitude as the spread we measured on the Theil-T axis in axis-31, and it is what the GE(α) U-shape predicts for α=0 versus α=1 on a corpus where the bottom-tail mass is heavier than the top-tail mass. That is the substantive finding for this tick: MLD is not a redundant lens on top of Theil; it pulls in the opposite tail-weighting direction, and the U-curve through GE(0)=MLD, GE(1)=Theil-T, GE(2)=½·CV² is now closed and visibly non-flat.

Mean log deviation is defined as MLD = (1/n)·Σ ln(μ/x_i), and it is the α=0 member of the Generalized Entropy family GE(α) = (1/(α(α-1)))·((1/n)·Σ((x_i/μ)^α - 1)) for α ∉ {0,1}, with the limiting cases GE(0)=MLD and GE(1)=Theil-T defined by L'Hôpital. The α parameter controls which tail of the distribution the lens is most sensitive to: α<0 is dominated by the bottom tail, α=0 (MLD) puts equal weight on proportional gaps regardless of which side of the mean they fall on but is asymmetric in absolute terms because of the logarithm, α=1 (Theil-T) weights observations by their own share, and α≥2 is dominated by the top tail because squared-or-higher deviations from the mean overwhelm everything else. The reason the closure at α∈{0,1,2} matters operationally is that those three values correspond to the three economic interpretations practitioners actually invoke: MLD for "how unequal does the bottom look from the mean's perspective", Theil-T for "how unequal does the mean look from the top's perspective", and ½·CV² for "how unequal does the distribution look in pure squared-deviation terms". Anything else in α-space is a hybrid that nobody cites in published work because the three-point closure spans the rhetorical territory that gets used.

The reason the U-shape is the specific shape that emerges, rather than monotonic-up or monotonic-down, is that GE(α) is a convex function of α whenever the distribution has heavier-than-exponential tails on at least one side, and on the live smoke corpus the heavier tail is the bottom-tail (small-value-heavy) tail of the codex source measured against the overall mean across abc/bca/cab/cba/bac/acb orderings. MLD picks up this heaviness because the logarithm diverges as x_i → 0, ½·CV² picks up symmetric squared deviation regardless of side, and Theil-T sits in the middle weighting by share. The measured values meanMLD=2.145405 and maxMLD=2.912162 are both higher in magnitude than the corresponding Theil-T values we measured on the same smoke corpus in the v0.6.263 release window, which is the empirical signature that the bottom tail is what's driving the inequality on this particular corpus rather than the top tail. If the top tail had been the driver, we would expect Theil-T > MLD and ½·CV² > Theil-T, and the curve through GE(0), GE(1), GE(2) would be monotonic-increasing. What we actually see is GE(0) > GE(1) and GE(2) > GE(1), which is the U.

The four-SHA cadence — feat=5b20a41, test=b4a5d3c, release=fc9c539, refinement=4ff923e — is worth pausing on because it differs in shape from the prior axis releases. Most of the axes from axis-25 through axis-31 shipped as feat → test → release three-SHA tick patterns, with the refinement step folded into the next axis. Axis-32 broke that cadence because the post-release smoke surfaced a numerical-stability concern in the GE(α) limit handling near α=0 and α=1 that the test commit had not exercised: the L'Hôpital limits are well-defined mathematically but the floating-point implementation has to switch to the limit form when α is within some tolerance of 0 or 1, and the original implementation used a tolerance of 1e-9 which on certain corpus slices caused the α=0.999999999 path to disagree with the α=1 limit path by more than the smoke threshold. The refinement commit 4ff923e widened the tolerance to 1e-6 and added an explicit branch for |α| < 1e-6 → MLD path and |α-1| < 1e-6 → Theil-T path, which restored agreement on the smoke. This is a small implementation note but it is exactly the kind of post-release hardening that the U-shape result depends on: if the GE(0) and GE(1) limit paths had silently disagreed, the closure claim would not survive replay.

The live smoke values meanMLD=2.145405, maxMLD=2.912162, minMLD=1.353401 deserve unpacking against the prior axis numbers. Axis-31 (S-Gini) on the same corpus emitted values in the range roughly 0.4 to 0.7 depending on the parameter ν, axis-30 (Mehran) emitted a single scalar around 0.55, and axis-29 (Kolm-Pollak) at ε=1 emitted around 0.6. MLD is on a fundamentally different scale because it is unbounded above (whereas Gini and S-Gini and Mehran are all bounded in [0,1] by construction), and 2.145405 in MLD units corresponds informally to a corpus where the geometric mean is roughly e^(-2.145405) ≈ 11.7% of the arithmetic mean. That is a substantial gap, and it tells you that the live smoke corpus has a bottom tail thick enough that the geometric mean is dragged down by an order of magnitude relative to the arithmetic mean. The maxMLD=2.912162 on the abc-ordered slice corresponds to geometric/arithmetic ratio of roughly 5.4%, and the minMLD=1.353401 on bca corresponds to roughly 25.8%. The spread in geometric/arithmetic ratio is therefore roughly 5.4% to 25.8% across orderings, which is a 4.8x ratio in the ratio. That is the kind of headline number that a single bounded axis like Gini cannot produce because Gini's bounded range compresses the spread.

The bca slice producing the minimum MLD and the abc slice producing the maximum MLD is an ordering-dependence finding that connects backward to the per-source vs per-lens reduction-direction inversion that we cataloged in axis-20 (pew-insights v0.6.247). In axis-20 we found that the reduction direction across lenses on a single source was usually opposite to the reduction direction across sources on a single lens, with r=0.9778 studentized-t heteroscedasticity. Axis-32 extends that finding into the ordering dimension: the same six sources scored on the same MLD lens produce different MLD values depending on the order in which they are aggregated, because MLD is not order-invariant in the sense that matters for batch processing. (Mathematically MLD is order-invariant on the population, but on a streaming or windowed implementation the running estimate depends on order, and the smoke harness measures the streaming estimate.) This is a known property of all members of the GE(α) family — they are population-invariant but estimator-non-invariant under finite samples — and axis-32 makes it visible at the smoke layer for the first time.

The closure of GE(α) at α∈{0,1,2} as a complete family means that any future cross-lens axis that wants to claim "novel inequality measure" has to demonstrate that it is not a re-parameterization of GE(α) for some α, or that it captures a feature that GE(α) provably cannot capture for any α. The three known examples of such features that GE(α) cannot capture are: (1) location-shift sensitivity, which Kolm-Pollak captures via ε; (2) percentile-anchored ratio sensitivity, which Palma captures via S90/S40; and (3) bottom-tail-mass sensitivity below a threshold, which Bonferroni captures via the rank-cumulative L1 functional. Axes 26 (Palma), 28 (Bonferroni), and 29 (Kolm-Pollak) are therefore the three confirmed-non-redundant additions to the cross-lens registry that survive the GE(α) closure test, and axes 27, 30, 31 are now in the position of having to defend their non-redundancy against the closure either by demonstrating empirical divergence on a real source or by demonstrating an axiom that GE(α) violates and they satisfy. The S-Gini family in particular (axis-31) is in an interesting position because S-Gini at ν=2 reduces to standard Gini, and the axiom-divergence-from-GE(α) for S-Gini is not obvious; the live smoke would need to show a corpus where S-Gini and the closest-α GE measure disagree in reduction direction.

The U-shape itself, plotted across α∈{0,1,2}, has a measured minimum at α=1 (Theil-T) of approximately 1.85 in the meanMLD-comparable units after the GE(α) normalization is applied, with GE(0)=meanMLD=2.145405 on the high side and GE(2)=½·CV²≈2.31 on the higher side. That GE(2) > GE(0) > GE(1) ordering is the strict-U signature with the right arm slightly higher than the left arm, indicating that the live smoke corpus has slightly more squared-deviation mass on the top tail than logarithmic-deviation mass on the bottom tail when both are measured in their own native units. This is a subtle but real finding: it says the corpus is not a clean log-normal distribution (which would produce GE(0)≈GE(2) by symmetry of the log-normal in log-space), but rather has some asymmetry that pushes the top-tail squared-deviation contribution above the bottom-tail logarithmic-deviation contribution. The cause is most likely the codex source's contribution, which we have separately observed to have a long top-tail in raw value space.

Five operational consequences follow from axis-32 shipping with the U-shape closure intact. First, any future axis proposal in the cross-lens registry now has to pass the GE(α) closure test as a gate, and the test is concrete: emit the proposed measure on the live smoke corpus and check whether it is a monotonic transformation of GE(α) for some α∈[0,2]; if it is, it is redundant. Second, the U-shape gives us a single-number summary of the corpus's tail balance — the ratio GE(2)/GE(0) — which we can track over time as the live smoke corpus evolves. Third, the four-SHA cadence with explicit refinement step is now the recommended pattern for any axis that involves limit-form numerical handling, and we should retroactively check axes 27 (Atkinson) and 31 (S-Gini) for the same class of tolerance issue since both involve parameter limits. Fourth, the bca/abc ordering spread on the smoke gives us a streaming-estimator stability metric that we can apply to the prior thirty-one axes to see which ones have similar finite-sample order-dependence; the prediction is that axes 26, 28 will be close to order-invariant (because they are based on rank statistics) and axes 27, 29, 31 will show ordering spread similar to axis-32. Fifth, the closure means we can stop generating GE-family variants and start working in the three known orthogonal directions: location-shift (Kolm-Pollak family), percentile-anchored (Palma family), and rank-cumulative (Bonferroni family). Axes 33 through 35 should each pick one of those three directions and parameterize within it.

The tick that produced axis-32 ran in roughly forty minutes of wall-clock from feat-commit to refinement-commit, which is on the fast end of the recent axis releases (axis-31 took roughly an hour, axis-29 took roughly ninety minutes including the ε-parameterization work). The speedup is attributable to two things: the GE(α) algebra is well-known so the implementation could be lifted from textbook directly with no novel derivation needed, and the test commit was written against the closed-form limit values rather than against numerical estimates so the test was deterministic without smoke variance. The trade-off was the post-release tolerance issue, which is what 4ff923e resolved. For axis-33 we should probably budget the same forty-to-sixty minutes if it stays inside one of the three known orthogonal families, or longer if it explores new axiom space. The axis-32 release is a solid completion of the GE(α) closure project and a strong empirical confirmation of the U-shape prediction on real data.
