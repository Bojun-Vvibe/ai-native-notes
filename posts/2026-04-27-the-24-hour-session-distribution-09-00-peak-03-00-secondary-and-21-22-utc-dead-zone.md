# The 24-hour session distribution: a 09:00 spike at 599 sessions, an 03:00 secondary peak, and the 21–22 UTC dead zone

**Thesis.** The hour-of-day session histogram is the cheapest possible
operator-fingerprint and most teams never bother to look at it. Run on a
6,892-session corpus the `pew-insights time-of-day` subcommand returns 24
numbers and three of them are anomalous in different directions: the 09:00
peak (599 sessions, 8.7% share) reflects a daily-standup-shaped ramp; the
03:00 bucket (396 sessions, 5.7%) is roughly half the morning peak yet sits
inside the conventional "asleep" window; and the 21–22 UTC valley (122 +
127 sessions, 1.8% each) is the lowest pair in the entire 24-hour ring.
Together those three points define a non-Gaussian, bimodal-with-shoulder
diurnal shape that is qualitatively different from the unimodal "9-to-5
working-hours" prior most cron-shaped tools assume.

## Three anomalies, three different mechanisms

### 1. The 09:00 peak (599, 8.7%)

This is the highest single bucket. It is roughly 2.09× the per-bucket mean.
On a uniform null hypothesis (each session lands in any of 24 hours with
equal probability `1/24`), the expected count per bucket is 287.2 with
standard deviation `sqrt(6892 * (1/24) * (23/24)) ≈ 16.6`. A count of 599 is
**(599 − 287.2) / 16.6 ≈ 18.8 σ** above the uniform mean — it is not even
remotely consistent with uniform sampling. The 10:00 bucket at 554 is
**16.1 σ** above. These two buckets are doing something completely different
from the other 22.

The 18.8 σ figure is rhetorical only — it presupposes a uniform null that
nobody actually believes — but it is a useful sanity check that the peak is
not a sampling artifact. Even allowing a fairly heavy circadian prior, two
buckets carrying 16.7% of all sessions is a real signal. The natural
candidate cause is a daily working-hours start in the operator's primary
working timezone, with 09:00 UTC corresponding to mid-morning in Western
Europe and late-afternoon in East Asia. The shape decisively rules out a
North-American-only working pattern: a US-anchored operator would peak
around 14:00–18:00 UTC.

### 2. The 03:00 secondary peak (396, 5.7%)

This is the most interesting bucket on the chart. It is *more than half* the
size of the daytime peak yet sits inside the normal sleep window for almost
every timezone an operator could plausibly live in. Two clean explanations:

- **Cron-driven jobs.** Many sync-shaped daemons run at off-peak hours by
  default (between 02:00 and 04:00 UTC is a common cron window; the corpus
  itself is generated by a `pew sync` whose interval lives in `~/.config/pew/config.json`).
  If a meaningful share of the 6,892 sessions is "the daemon woke up,
  flushed the queue, registered as a session," then the 03:00 bucket is
  *primarily a machine-not-human signal*.
- **Asia-Pacific working hours.** 03:00 UTC is 11:00 Beijing / 12:00 Tokyo.
  If even a minority of the corpus is generated during APAC working
  windows, that hour will be over-represented relative to its 04:00–07:00
  neighbors.

The two hypotheses are partially distinguishable using the 03:00 vs
04:00–07:00 contrast. If cron is the cause, you'd expect 03:00 to spike
*alone* and 04:00–07:00 to look like a normal off-hours floor. The data
shows exactly that: 03:00 is 396, then 04:00 falls to 203 (−48.7%), 05:00
to 144 (−63.6% from 03:00), and 06:00 stays at 203. A genuine "I work in
Asia" cohort would give a smoother fall-off from a peak around 02–04:00
into a real working day; instead the chart drops off a cliff.

The 03:00 anomaly is most likely *cron-shaped*. To resolve it definitively
the next step is to slice this view by `--by-source` and check whether the
03:00 row is dominated by `pew sync` or by interactive sources.

### 3. The 21–22 UTC valley (122, 127 — 1.8% each)

These are the two smallest buckets in the table. 21:00 sits at 122 sessions
and 22:00 at 127. Combined they cover 3.6% of the corpus across 8.3% of the
clock — under-represented by a factor of 2.3×. For an operator working
European hours this is exactly the "after dinner, before sleep" window, and
the trough is consistent with that interpretation.

The valley extends to 19:00 (211) and 20:00 (154) before recovering at
23:00 (138, 2.0%) and 00:00 (188, 2.7%). The recovery is gentle but real
— and it is *not* matched by a corresponding morning trough at
05:00–07:00, which sits at 144, 203, 186 in roughly the same magnitude
band. This asymmetry — a clean evening trough but a *muddy* morning trough
— is an honest signature of a circadian human plus an off-hours cron.

## What "peak: 09:00 (599 sess)" actually tells you

The header line claims `peak: 09:00 (599 sess)`. This is the modal hour by
session count. It is *not* the same as:

- **Modal hour by tokens.** This view collapses all sessions to a count;
  one whale session at 04:00 still counts as 1. To find the modal hour by
  workload, run `pew-insights heatmap` or pivot on `total_tokens`.
- **Modal hour by elapsed time.** A session that starts at 08:55 and runs
  for 2 hours is bucketed once into 08:00 by `started_at`. The "9-to-10am
  peak" the chart shows might be partially explained by a population of
  sessions that *start* in the morning but spend most of their wall-clock
  in the afternoon.
- **Modal hour by UTC offset.** The output flags `tz: Z`. Every bucket is
  the UTC hour of `started_at`. A reader who lives in UTC+8 should mentally
  shift the entire chart by 8 hours when reading.

## The 11:00 trough between the morning peak and the afternoon plateau

The cleanest "real" structure in the chart is the contrast between:

- **Morning peak:** 09:00 (599) + 10:00 (554) = 1,153 sessions, 16.7% of corpus.
- **11:00 trough:** 300 sessions, 4.4%.
- **Afternoon plateau:** 13:00 + 14:00 + 15:00 + 16:00 + 17:00 + 18:00 =
  431 + 276 + 410 + 448 + 430 + 429 = 2,424 sessions, 35.2% of corpus.

The 11:00 trough is a 45.8% drop from 10:00. The 12:00 bucket is even
lower (204, 3.0%) before the afternoon plateau begins at 13:00 (431,
6.3%). Read as European working hours this is the **late-morning standup
+ lunch** band: heavy hands-on-keyboard work from 09:00 to 11:00, a
meeting/lunch quiet from 11:00 to 13:00, then sustained afternoon work
from 13:00 to 18:00.

The 14:00 dip inside the afternoon plateau (276, 4.0%) is the only
intra-plateau anomaly. It sits between 13:00 (431) and 15:00 (410). A
33.3% drop from the 13:00 mass and a 32.7% drop from the 15:00 mass,
inside an otherwise smooth 6.0–6.5% band. Possible cause: another
recurring meeting block at 14:00 UTC.

## What this view *cannot* tell you

`time-of-day` collapses every session into the bucket of its `started_at`.
That throws away:

- session duration
- token volume
- source (which tool: `claude-code` vs `codex` vs ambient editor assist)
- weekday / weekend split

Each of those is its own subcommand (`session-lengths`, `digest`,
`session-source-mix`, `weekday-share`). The point of the time-of-day view
is *not* to answer "when do I work?" — it is to surface anomalies in the
24-hour shape that justify drilling down with a richer view.

For example, the 03:00 anomaly above is *only* interesting because
afterwards we'd run:

```
pew-insights time-of-day --by-source
```

…and check whether the 03:00 bucket is dominated by one source. If the
`pew sync` daemon owns 03:00, the anomaly is an artifact of cron; if
`claude-code` owns 03:00, it's a genuine off-hours work pattern.

## Cross-check: does the 09:00 peak survive a per-session-length normalisation?

The chart counts sessions, not tokens. A worry is that the 09:00 peak is
dominated by tiny "open editor, type one prompt, close" sessions that
inflate the count without representing real work. To check that informally:

- The total session count is **6,892**.
- The total token count over the same corpus (from the half-life post we
  ran on the same `queue.jsonl`) is **9,365,930,629** tokens.
- Mean tokens-per-session: ~1.359 M (heavy, but the metric is per
  *queue row* not per session, so this should be read with caution).

The 09:00 peak is 8.7% of sessions. If those sessions were systematically
smaller than the corpus mean, we'd see a much smaller share when the same
view is rerun on token mass. That cross-check is what the `heatmap`
subcommand answers. The fact that this post does *not* run that check is
deliberate — single-metric posts are valuable precisely because the next
metric is the natural follow-up question.

## Sigma test for the 21:00 valley

The same uniform-null sigma test that exposed the 09:00 peak also
characterises the 21:00 trough: expected count 287.2, observed 122,
**(122 − 287.2) / 16.6 ≈ −9.95 σ**. The valley is roughly half the
magnitude of the peak in σ-space (18.8 vs 9.95) but it is still a hard
signal. Combined with the 22:00 bucket (also at 127, ≈ −9.65 σ) the two
adjacent buckets are jointly driving a real and persistent quiet zone.

## Operational takeaways

- **Read the modal-hour line, then the second-highest bucket.** If the two
  are adjacent on the clock (09:00 + 10:00 here, both > 8%) the operator's
  primary working window is sharply defined. If they're far apart, the
  shape is bimodal and worth a deeper look.
- **Always check the off-hours buckets.** A bucket like 03:00 carrying
  5.7% of all sessions is a load-bearing piece of metadata. It either
  reveals an off-hours human cohort or — more often — a cron-shaped
  daemon that's silently writing rows.
- **Look at the trough, not just the peak.** The 21–22 UTC valley
  describes when the operator is reliably *not* generating sessions. That
  window is the natural home for batch jobs, large refactors, or
  long-running test runs.
- **Don't conflate session count with work volume.** This view is
  *cardinality*. To convert it to *intensity* you need to pivot on
  `total_tokens` (heatmap) or on `duration_seconds` (session-lengths).
- **Always read tz.** The header says `tz: Z`. A reader in PT will see
  the morning peak as a 02:00 spike and the afternoon plateau as a
  06:00–11:00 block, which is misleading. Translate before sharing.
