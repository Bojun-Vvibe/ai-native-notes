# Diurnal output-CV by source: codex swings 0.93, openclaw holds 0.29, and what that says about reply-shape stability

*as of `2026-04-26T22:29:22.885Z` — `pew-insights source-output-tokens-by-hour-cv` over 9.45 billion total tokens and 1,600 active hour-buckets from `~/.config/pew/queue.jsonl`*

## A different CV, on a different axis

The gap-CV metric ranks sources by the *rhythm* of when they fire. This one ranks them by the *size* of what they emit, and specifically by how stable that size is across the 24 hours of the day. The arithmetic is:

```
hourCv = stddev(per-hour mean output_tokens) / mean of same
```

For each source, the algorithm bins all rows by UTC hour-of-day (0..23), computes the mean per-row `output_tokens` inside each populated hour, then takes the CV across those up-to-24 means. High `hourCv` means a source's typical reply size is itself a function of the time of day. Low `hourCv` means a source's reply size looks the same regardless of when it fires.

## Reading the column

The story is best told in three brackets.

**Stable reply shape (hourCv ≤ 0.40).** `openclaw` (0.2946), `opencode` (0.3708), and `hermes` (0.3728). All three look the same regardless of UTC hour. openclaw is the most stable: its mean per-hour mean output is 11,547 tokens with a standard deviation of only 3,402 across all 24 hours. Whether you query openclaw at 03:00 UTC or at 17:00 UTC, the typical row size is within ±30% of the same number. opencode is similar at a much higher level (mean 69,117 tokens per hour-mean, std 25,629 — a ~37% spread). hermes is the lowest-volume of the three (mean 7,746) but maintains the same flat profile.

The thing all three of these have in common is *automation*. They are dispatcher-driven sources whose work is generated by a scheduler, not a human keyboard, and the scheduler does not particularly care what time of day it is. The reply size is determined by the *task type* (summarise this, lint that, dispatch the other), and the task-type mix is roughly stationary across the day. That stationarity is exactly what hourCv detects.

**Mixed (hourCv 0.47 – 0.64).** `ide-assistant-A` (0.4700) and `claude-code` (0.6410). The former is the editor-side assistant — high row count (333) but tiny token mass (1.13M output tokens total). It is technically attended, but the per-row outputs are small and bounded by what fits in an inline completion. It still picks up some diurnal variation, but it cannot swing very far because its absolute reply sizes are capped. claude-code is much closer to the bursty end: mean 72,072 tokens per hour-mean, std 46,197 — a 64% spread across the day. claude-code is the heavyweight attended-but-sometimes-automated source on this machine, and the 0.64 CV reflects that: it picks up automation-shaped reply sizes during some hours and human-shaped reply sizes during others.

**Highly variable reply shape (hourCv ≥ 0.90).** `codex` alone, at 0.9284. Mean per-hour mean output 37,612, standard deviation 34,918 — the std is essentially the same magnitude as the mean. This is what near-Poisson dispersion looks like on the size axis: codex's typical reply at one populated hour is 3,000 tokens; at another it is 80,000. The hour-of-day isn't *causing* this — what is causing it is that codex has only 16 populated hours out of 24 (the lowest of any source in the table), and inside those 16 hours the workload mix swings between "answer a one-liner" and "draft a complete refactor."

`hoursPop = 16` is the secret of codex's high CV. With only 16 buckets to average over, each bucket carries more weight in the standard deviation, and the few hours with monster generations dominate. If codex were active 24/7 the hour-mean curve would smooth out. It isn't, so it doesn't.

## The denominator nuance

A subtle but important property of this metric: it is not weighted by how many rows landed in each hour. The denominator inside each hour bucket is the number of *rows* in that hour (call it `n_h`), used to compute that hour's mean output. The CV across hours then treats every populated hour equally, regardless of whether it had 4 rows or 40.

That is intentional — we want the *shape of the day*, not the *mass of the day*. If we weighted by row count, an hour with 100 small rows would dominate an hour with 5 huge rows, even though the *typical* reply at the busy hour is small and the *typical* reply at the quiet hour is huge. The unweighted version preserves that signal.

It does, however, mean that an hour with a single freak row can move the per-hour mean (and therefore the CV) noticeably. The mitigation is that all six sources have at least 64 rows total (codex) and most have several hundred, spread across many hours. Single-row hours exist but cannot dominate.

If you want the row-weighted version, that is essentially `output-size` (per-model row distribution percentiles) collapsed across all hours. The two metrics report different things and they should not be expected to agree. `output-size` tells you the *centre and spread* of reply sizes; `source-output-tokens-by-hour-cv` tells you whether that centre wanders across the clock.

## Why this is not redundant with neighbouring metrics

There are five superficially-similar metrics in the report. Lay them out and the orthogonality becomes clear:

- **`hour-of-day-token-skew`**: third moment of the global token mass curve over hours-of-day. Single global number, not per-source. Tells you the *skewness* of the day, not the *variability* of reply size within it.
- **`source-token-mass-hour-centroid`**: per-source circular mean of the active-hour distribution. Tells you *when* the source's mass concentrates, not *how variable* the reply size is across hours.
- **`source-hour-of-day-token-mass-entropy`**: per-source Shannon entropy of token-mass distribution across hours. Tells you how *spread out* the mass is, but mass — not per-row reply size.
- **`source-hour-of-day-topk-mass-share`**: per-source share of mass concentrated in the K busiest hours. Concentration scalar on mass.
- **`source-output-tokens-per-row-percentiles`**: per-source p50/p90/p99 of single-row output. Collapses the hour axis entirely.

None of these answers the question "does the typical reply at 09:00 UTC look like the typical reply at 21:00 UTC?" That is what diurnal output-CV is for. It is the only metric in the family that holds the *per-row size* as the variable of interest while reading it as a function of *hour-of-day*. Both axes matter. Drop either one and you collapse into one of the neighbouring metrics.

## What 0.93 actually means for codex

A CV near 1 on a per-row size axis is large enough to be operationally relevant.

Concretely: if codex's mean per-hour mean output is 37,612 tokens and the std across hours is 34,918, then a one-sigma swing from the mean spans roughly 2,700 tokens to 72,500 tokens. **The "typical" reply size at codex's busiest hour is more than 25× the "typical" reply size at codex's quietest hour.** That is not a measurement quirk — that is two qualitatively different workloads being routed through the same source name on the same machine.

If you were trying to predict the cost of the next codex call from the time of day, you would do better than chance — and meaningfully better. The same prediction for openclaw would barely beat the prior: the per-hour mean varies by ±30%, which gives you almost no leverage on a single call.

This is where diurnal CV becomes a usable forecasting signal. It is also where it becomes a usable *anomaly* signal: a source whose hourCv suddenly jumps from 0.30 to 0.70 has changed its workload mix, even if its total mass is unchanged. You will not see that change in any of the mass-axis metrics, because total mass is invariant under shape changes that preserve the integral. The CV on per-row-size will see it instantly.

## The hoursPop column hides a story

`hoursPop` (the count of UTC hours-of-day with at least one row) is worth reading carefully. Five of the six sources hit 24 (or close to it: claude-code at 20, ide-assistant-A at 14). Codex sits at 16.

Codex's 16 populated hours are concentrated in working hours UTC, with the rest of the clock dark. That sparseness is by itself an explanation for half of codex's CV — fewer hours to average over means the law of large numbers helps less, and the per-hour means are noisier.

If you wanted to ask "is codex's per-row size diurnally unstable beyond what sample-size noise would predict?" the right control would be to bootstrap-resample claude-code's 20 populated hours down to 16 and see what hourCv you get on average. That bootstrap is left as an exercise; the headline observation here is that the raw 0.93 CV is partly a workload-shape signal and partly a sample-size signal, and the two are entangled.

The other extreme — ide-assistant-A's 14 populated hours — does not produce a high CV, because ide-assistant-A's per-row outputs are tightly capped by the inline-completion form factor. With or without sample-size noise, the variability of a 200-token cap-driven reply size is small. ide-assistant-A's hourCv of 0.47 is the genuine signal of "the editor source has a mild diurnal pattern but cannot swing far because its outputs are bounded."

## Cross-product with the gap-hours CV ranking

It is irresistible to lay the two CV rankings side by side:

```
source           gapCv   hourCv
---------------  ------  ------
claude-code      3.7369  0.6410
ide-assistant-A  3.1083  0.4700
codex            1.7004  0.9284
hermes           0.7839  0.3728
openclaw         0.6498  0.2946
opencode         0.5977  0.3708
```

Three observations.

**(1)** The two top-ranked sources by gap-CV (claude-code, ide-assistant-A) are in the middle of the hour-CV ranking. They have heavy-tailed gap distributions but only mildly heavy-tailed reply-size distributions across hours. The "vacation" pattern dominates their gap geometry but is *invisible* to hourCv (which collapses the date axis entirely).

**(2)** Codex is the only source where the hour-CV ranking *exceeds* the gap-CV ranking on the relative scale. codex's gapCv of 1.70 puts it in the middle of the gap-rhythm ranking, but its hourCv of 0.93 puts it at the top of the reply-size diurnal ranking. The two CVs are picking up two different regularities, and codex is the source that sits in the middle on rhythm but at the extreme on shape.

**(3)** The three daemons (hermes, openclaw, opencode) cluster at the bottom of *both* rankings. Whatever automation is driving them produces both a regular cadence (low gapCv) and a stable reply size across the day (low hourCv). The two metrics agree about who the daemons are.

This is the kind of cross-product that motivates building both metrics: the agreement between them on the daemon group confirms each one's validity, and the disagreement on codex tells you something real about codex specifically.

## What I'm taking away

1. **The diurnal output-CV ranking is 3.15× wide across six sources.** Openclaw at 0.2946 to codex at 0.9284. That spread is enough to cluster sources into stable / mixed / variable reply-shape regimes without any further structure.

2. **The metric is genuinely orthogonal to all four neighbouring hour-of-day stats.** Mass concentration, mass entropy, top-K mass share, and circular centroid all read mass, not per-row size. Only this metric reads per-row size as a function of hour-of-day.

3. **codex's 0.93 is partly workload, partly sample size.** With only 16 populated hours and a workload that swings between one-liner answers and full refactors, codex's per-hour means are inherently noisy. The headline number deserves a sample-size caveat, which neither the metric nor the table currently surfaces.

4. **Daemons are stable, attended sources mix, codex is the outlier.** Same conclusion as the gap-hours CV ranking, on a different axis. The two metrics agreeing on the daemons and disagreeing on codex is more informative than either one alone.

5. **A useful forecasting signal.** A CV near 1 means time-of-day predicts reply size well above chance. A CV near 0.3 means it barely predicts at all. That is an operationally usable distinction — it tells you whether to bother building hour-aware capacity plans for a given source.

Next pass I want to do is the date-ordered version: replace "UTC hour-of-day 0..23" with "active calendar day 1..N" inside each source, and watch the CV evolve. The hypothesis is that hourCv will be reasonably stable for the daemons (they don't change much week to week) and will drift for the attended sources as the workload mix changes. If hourCv is drifting upward week-over-week for a source you care about, it means that source's workload is bifurcating — and that bifurcation deserves a closer look at *what* the workload is doing, not just *how much* of it there is.
