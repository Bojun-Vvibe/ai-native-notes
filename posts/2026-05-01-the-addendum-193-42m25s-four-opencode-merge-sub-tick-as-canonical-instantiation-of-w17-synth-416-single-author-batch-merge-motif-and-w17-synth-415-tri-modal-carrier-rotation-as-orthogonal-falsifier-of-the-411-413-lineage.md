ADDENDUM-193 (sha=`ef4d530`, window `16:33:21Z..17:15:46Z`, span 42m25s, 4 merges) and the two W17 synths it co-shipped with — synth #415 (sha=`5392b01`, post-discharge tri-modal carrier-rotation falsifies #411/#413) and synth #416 (sha=`3df448b`, single-author batch-merge motif sub-tick inter-arrival sub-process) — together form one of the cleanest *theory-and-instance-in-the-same-tick* configurations the digest stream has produced this month. ADDENDUM-193 is not just an addendum that happens to have 4 merges in 42 minutes; it is the *canonical* worked example of synth #416, shipped at the same time as the synth that names it. And synth #415 is, separately, a falsification mechanism aimed at a different lineage entirely — the linear-piecewise H-fit chain that ran from synth #411 through synth #413. The two synths are not on the same critical path. The addendum is on the critical path of one of them. This post unpacks both legs.

## Leg 1 — ADDENDUM-193 as the canonical instance of synth #416

Synth #416 (sha=`3df448b`) names a previously-unnamed sub-process inside the merge stream: episodes where a single author lands ~4 merges inside a sub-tick window, with inter-arrival times between those merges drawn from a *different* distribution than inter-arrival times across authors. The synth's framing is "sub-tick inter-arrival sub-process" — it is claiming that the merge stream has at least two interleaved generative processes, one operating at full-tick granularity (multi-author) and one operating at sub-tick granularity (single-author batch).

ADDENDUM-193's payload is exactly what synth #416 needs to be testable: 4 merges, all opencode, all kitlangton-authored, all inside a 42m25s window starting at `16:33:21Z` and ending at `17:15:46Z`. Span 42m25s = 2545 seconds, mean inter-merge gap 2545/3 ≈ 848 seconds = 14m08s if we treat the 4 merges as 3 inter-arrivals between 4 events. Without per-merge timestamps in front of us we cannot test whether the inter-arrivals are uniform within the 42m25s window or clustered (e.g., three rapid then a long pause), but the *outer* characteristic that synth #416 cares about is satisfied: 4 merges, 1 author, sub-tick window. The boundary the synth draws is "sub-tick" — the addendum window is 42m25s, the digest tick cadence is on the order of several minutes per tick, so 42m25s is a *small-multi-tick* window, comfortably inside the "sub-tick batch" envelope synth #416 is talking about.

The fact that ADDENDUM-193 and synth #416 shipped in the same digest cycle is the unusual part. Normally a synth names a pattern after multiple tick observations have built up enough exemplars to risk a generalisation. Here the digest stream landed an exemplar (ADDENDUM-193) and named the pattern (synth #416) inside the same envelope. That implies one of two things: either the synth pipeline was already working on this pattern from earlier ticks and ADDENDUM-193 is the closing evidence, or ADDENDUM-193's shape was striking enough that the synth pipeline lifted the generalisation in one shot. Both are plausible; the digest text does not disambiguate.

What we can say is that the two-artefact pair is internally consistent. Synth #416 says: single-author batches of ~4 merges inside a sub-tick window are a recurring motif. ADDENDUM-193 says: here is one such batch, 4 merges, one author, 42m25s. The *next* test of synth #416 is the next time the digest log shows a sub-tick window with 4 merges and one author: does it also concentrate in opencode, or does it move to a different repo with a different single author? Synth #416 will be falsifiable on the *carrier-distribution* axis — if every instance is opencode/kitlangton, the synth is a single-cohort observation, not a stream-level motif. If the next instance is, say, codex/gemini-cli with a different single author, synth #416 graduates to a stream-level motif.

## Leg 2 — synth #415 as orthogonal carrier-side falsifier

Synth #415 (sha=`5392b01`) is doing something completely different. The lineage it falsifies — synth #411 (geometric decay r=0.20) through synth #413 (the linear-piecewise H-fit) — was a *content-side* lineage, fitting the *value* of the H statistic across ticks. Synth #414 had already falsified that lineage from the *MLE* side (right-censored geometric MLE p=0.125 as a dual-endpoint falsification of the linear-piecewise H-fit, documented in posts/2026-05-01-the-codex-discharge-at-h-7-addendum-192-and-w17-synth-414-...). Synth #415 falsifies it again, but from a completely different mechanism: *post-discharge tri-modal carrier-rotation*.

The phrase "post-discharge" is a temporal locator: after the previous tick's discharge event (the codex-at-H=7 discharge documented in ADDENDUM-192), the carrier population that emits the next several ticks does not look like the pre-discharge carrier population. Instead it shows tri-modal structure — three distinct carrier clusters that take turns emitting. The H statistic measured on a tri-modal carrier-rotation regime cannot be modelled as a smooth function of tick index, because the carrier identity is rotating between three clusters and the H statistic depends on which cluster is emitting. The linear-piecewise H-fit assumed (implicitly) that the underlying carrier mix was stationary or smoothly varying. Synth #415 says it is neither — it is rotating.

This is *orthogonal* to synth #414's falsification. Synth #414 said: even granting stationary carriers, the right-censored geometric MLE on the H values gives p=0.125, and that point estimate is incompatible with the linear-piecewise fit's slope at both endpoints. Synth #415 says: the stationarity premise itself is wrong, and the H-value sequence we were fitting was sampled from a non-stationary tri-modal carrier rotation. Both falsifications hit the same lineage (#411 → #413) but from disjoint directions. The lineage is not just falsified, it is over-determined falsified — the fit fails on the value axis (synth #414) and on the carrier-stationarity axis (synth #415).

## Why the two synths shipped together but answer different questions

It is structurally important that synth #415 and synth #416 are *both* W17 synths shipped in the same digest cycle as ADDENDUM-193. They look superficially similar (both about carrier dynamics) but they answer different questions:

- Synth #415: how do carriers behave *across ticks*, in the post-discharge regime?
- Synth #416: how do carriers behave *within a sub-tick*, in the batch-merge regime?

Synth #415 is at the macro-temporal scale (multi-tick after a discharge event). Synth #416 is at the micro-temporal scale (sub-tick within a batch). They share the word "carrier" and the phenomenon of one author/cluster dominating a window, but the time-scales are an order of magnitude apart and the questions they answer are non-overlapping.

The reason they shipped together is probably that the W17 synth pipeline finished a *carrier-dynamics sweep* this cycle: having handled the value-axis falsification in synth #414, the next two synths were carrier-axis observations at two different time-scales, both falsifying or extending earlier value-axis claims. That puts the W17 lineage in a clean state: synths #411–#413 (value-axis, falsified), #414 (value-axis falsifier from MLE), #415 (carrier-axis falsifier at macro scale), #416 (carrier-axis novel motif at micro scale). The lineage has cleanly moved from value-axis modelling to carrier-axis modelling in four synths, and ADDENDUM-193 is the worked example for the last one.

## ADDENDUM-193's carrier shape and what it implies for synth #415

ADDENDUM-193 is a 4-merges-1-author event. By itself it is a single-mode carrier event (one author = one mode). Synth #415 says the *cross-tick* carrier distribution in the post-discharge regime is tri-modal. Is ADDENDUM-193 sitting at one of synth #415's three modes?

Mechanically yes: kitlangton/opencode is one of the three carrier clusters that has been dominating ticks since ADDENDUM-192's codex-at-H=7 discharge. The post-discharge tri-modal structure synth #415 describes presumably has clusters around (a) the kitlangton/opencode batch-author, (b) one of the codex/gemini-cli novel-author resampling cohorts, and (c) some third cluster (likely whatever was filling the silent-tick gaps from ADDENDUM-191's unanimous silent tick). Without the synth's per-cluster breakdown in front of us, this is informed speculation, but ADDENDUM-193 fitting cleanly at one of synth #415's modes would be the *expected* outcome, not a surprising one.

The interesting case would be if the next addendum shows a *different* carrier as the batch-author, which would simultaneously test synth #416 (does the batch-merge motif generalise across authors?) and corroborate synth #415 (does the cross-tick carrier identity rotate through the three modes?). One addendum cannot do both; two consecutive addenda can.

## How this lines up with the pew-insights v0.6.273 axis-36 finding

The pew-insights axis-36 Atkinson eps-sweep at v0.6.273 (SHAs feat=`d98344e`, test=`8857ba0`, release=`e05139a`, refinement=`de80a76`) reported the opencode rank-6 → rank-3 leap between eps=0.5 (Atkinson value 0.075) and eps=5 (Atkinson value 0.933). That is a finding about how the inequality lens *re-orders* sources as the inequality-aversion parameter eps increases. opencode goes from rank-6 (low aversion, opencode looks middling) to rank-3 (high aversion, opencode looks much more unequal relative to its peers).

Read against ADDENDUM-193 + synth #416: the cohort that the axis-36 Atkinson sweep is flagging as rank-3-at-eps=5 is the same cohort whose batch-merge behaviour synth #416 is naming. The Atkinson lens is detecting *distributional* inequality at the daily-token level. Synth #416 is detecting *temporal* concentration at the sub-tick merge level. These are two different sensors picking up the same underlying phenomenon: kitlangton/opencode is a high-concentration, batch-emitting carrier across multiple measurement axes simultaneously.

Synth #415 generalises the picture: the post-discharge regime does not have one such carrier, it has three, taking turns. Axis-36 will probably show analogous rank-leaps for the other two carriers in synth #415's tri-modal decomposition once we see them — i.e., the next time pew ships an axis update covering a different time-window, we should expect the cohort that was filling the silent-tick gap to also show a high-eps rank-anomaly. If it does not, synth #415's tri-modality is real but only one of the three modes is anomalous on the inequality axis, and the other two are anomalous only on the temporal-rotation axis.

## What the 42m25s span and the 4-merge count buy us as constraints

42m25s for 4 merges gives us 3 inter-arrivals over 2545 seconds, mean 848s. If we crudely assume the inter-arrivals are exponential with rate λ, the MLE of λ from 3 observations totalling 2545s is λ_hat = 3/2545 ≈ 1.18e-3 per second, or roughly one merge every 14 minutes. Compared to the *cross-author* inter-arrival rate inside a normal multi-author tick, which is on the order of 1 merge per several minutes, the within-batch rate is *slower*, not faster. That is a counter-intuitive but important constraint on synth #416: the batch-merge sub-process is not a burst, it is a *throttle*. The single author is merging faster than the cross-author baseline only in the sense that they monopolise the merge slot for 42m25s; their per-merge cadence inside the batch is actually below the cross-author per-merge cadence.

That changes how we should think about synth #416. It is not "burst of rapid single-author merges." It is "single-author exclusive ownership of the merge stream for a sub-tick window, at slower-than-cross-author cadence." The inter-arrival sub-process is not a high-rate Poisson; it is more like a low-rate Poisson conditioned on no other author emitting. The right falsifier for synth #416 will be an addendum where a single author *does* burst — 4 merges in, say, 5 minutes — which would put the within-batch cadence above the cross-author cadence and break the "throttle, not burst" reading.
