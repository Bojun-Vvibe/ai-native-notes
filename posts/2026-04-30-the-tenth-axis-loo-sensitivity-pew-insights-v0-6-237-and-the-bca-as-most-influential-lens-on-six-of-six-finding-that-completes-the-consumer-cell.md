# The tenth axis: LOO sensitivity in pew-insights v0.6.237, and the BCa-as-most-influential-lens on six-of-six finding that completes the consumer cell

## A decuple of consumer axes

The consumer-lens cell of pew-insights — the subsystem that does not produce a new slope CI, but instead consumes the six existing slope-CI lenses (percentile bootstrap, jackknife normal, BCa, studentized-t, ABC, profile-likelihood) and reports cross-lens disagreement — reached its tenth orthogonal axis on the dispatcher tick at `2026-04-29T21:07:55Z`. The recent history line for the tick records the bump verbatim: "feature shipped pew-insights v0.6.236->v0.6.237 source-row-token-slope-ci-leave-one-lens-out 10th cross-lens axis LOO sensitivity per-source jackknife-of-lenses on 6-lens consensus + refinement signedShift/direction tests 6456->6506 (+50: 44 release + 6 refinement) live-smoke 6 sources/all-lenses 6 meanLooStability=0.9149 medianLooStability=0.9268 bca=mostInfluentialLens for ALL 6 sources (3 up/3 down symmetric off-center not directional) bootstrap=leastInfluential for 4/6 HEAD=3b87a6d (4 commits 2 pushes 0 blocks all guardrails clean both pushes)".

## What the prior nine axes saw and did not see

The first nine axes of the cell are all descriptions of the joint geometry of the six per-source CIs as a static configuration. The CHANGELOG entry for v0.6.237 lists them in exactly that frame — "every prior axis describes the JOINT geometry of the six CIs as a STATIC configuration (signs, widths, overlaps, ranks, midpoints, asymmetries, IoUs)" — and then enumerates them: v0.6.227 jaccard (sign-set), v0.6.228 sign-concordance, v0.6.229 width-concordance, v0.6.230 overlap-graph (1-bit pair), v0.6.231 midpoint-dispersion (center-spread only), v0.6.232 asymmetry (single-CI shape), v0.6.233 pair-inclusion (5-category labels), v0.6.234 rank-correlation (cross-source ordinal), v0.6.235 coverage-volume (continuous pair IoU). Nine axes, nine views of the same six intervals.

None of those nine axes answers a question that an analyst presented with a six-lens consensus actually wants answered: **if I delete lens k, how much does the consensus midpoint move and how much does the consensus width change?** That is the influence-function question. It is the question a classical jackknife-of-estimators answers when you treat each lens as a unit of resampling. It is the question the tenth axis was built to answer.

The mechanical distinction is therefore real, not cosmetic. The first nine axes are functions of the static six-tuple of CIs. The tenth axis is a function of how the six-tuple decomposes when you remove its members one at a time. A configuration can score well on the first nine — narrow widths, concordant signs, high IoU coverage, unanimous ranking — and still be entirely driven by one hyper-precise lens whose midpoint anchors the consensus center. If that lens is wrong, the consensus is wrong, and the first nine axes will not see the failure. The tenth axis is the only one that pulls each lens out one at a time and says how much of the consensus depends on it.

## The mechanics of the LOO axis

The CHANGELOG describes the computation precisely. For each source, on the 6 CI midpoints `mid_k = (lo_k + hi_k) / 2` and the 6 widths `w_k = hi_k - lo_k`, the axis computes:

- `fullMid` / `fullWidth` — the mean of all 6 midpoints / widths. This is the consensus.
- `fullMidStd` — the sample standard deviation (with `n-1`) of the six midpoints. This is the spread of the lenses on the location axis, which axis 5 (midpoint-dispersion) already reports.
- Six `loo` rows, one per removed lens k:
  - `looMid` is the mean of the OTHER FIVE midpoints,
  - `looWidth` is the mean of the OTHER FIVE widths,
  - `midShift` is `|looMid - fullMid|` — the absolute distance the consensus midpoint moves when k is removed,
  - `signedMidShift` is `looMid - fullMid` — the signed version, where positive means removing k pulls the consensus UP (so k was dragging it DOWN), and negative means k was pulling it UP,
  - `widthShift` is `looWidth - fullWidth`,
  - `widthRatio` is `looWidth / fullWidth` — equal to 1 means k had no effect on width, greater than 1 means k was tighter than average (removing it widened the consensus), less than 1 means k was wider than average (removing it tightened the consensus),
  - `midShiftStd` is `midShift / fullWidth` — a unitless influence score, comparable across sources.

Per-source aggregates roll the six LOO rows into a single record: `mostInfluentialLens` (the k with the largest `midShiftStd`), `leastInfluentialLens`, `tightestLens` (largest `widthRatio` — its removal widens the consensus most, so it was the tightest contributor), `widestLens`, `maxMidShiftStd`, `meanMidShiftStd`, `widthRatioRange`, and the headline scalar `looStabilityScore = 1 / (1 + maxMidShiftStd)` in the half-open interval `(0, 1]`. A score of 1.0 means removing any single lens leaves the consensus midpoint exactly where it was. Anything strictly less than 1 means at least one lens has nonzero influence.

Report-level aggregates roll the per-source records into workspace scalars: `meanLooStability`, `medianLooStability`, `globalMostInfluentialLens` and `globalLeastInfluentialLens` (mode across sources). Edge cases are handled deterministically: `fullWidth == 0` forces `midShiftStd = 0` and `widthRatio = 1` by convention (not NaN); all six midpoints identical forces every `midShift = 0` and `looStabilityScore = 1`. The defaults are correct on degenerate inputs, which is the kind of detail that decides whether a diagnostic is usable in production.

The CHANGELOG quotes the verbatim live-smoke output against `~/.config/pew/queue.jsonl` at `2026-04-29T20:59:38.885Z`, with 2006 rows across 6 sources, default settings except `--bootstraps 500`. The summary line reports `dropped: 0 missing-from-some-lens, 0 above-alert-threshold; meanLooStability: 0.9149; medianLooStability: 0.9268; globalMostInfluentialLens: bca; globalLeastInfluentialLens: bootstrap`. Two patterns are visible at a glance. First: `bca` is the `mostInfluentialLens` for ALL SIX sources — every row of the `mostInf` column is `bca`. Removing BCa shifts the consensus midpoint more than removing any other lens, on every source in the corpus. Second: `bca` is also the `widestLens` for 4 of 6 sources (hermes, opencode, vscode-copilot, claude-code), meaning that removing BCa tightens the consensus width the most. So BCa is consistently both far from the other five lenses on the center axis AND much wider than them on the spread axis. That is the diagnostic signature of a lens whose acceleration / bias-correction step is overshooting on the local queue's small per-source sample sizes — the BCa correction term is doing real work, but the work it is doing is pulling its CI away from the other five.

The mirror finding: `bootstrap` (percentile) is the `leastInfluentialLens` for 4 of 6 sources (codex, openclaw, vscode-copilot, claude-code). Removing percentile bootstrap barely moves the consensus, because its midpoint and width sit close to the jackknife/studentizedT/abc/profileLikelihood cluster. Percentile bootstrap is the centroid lens of the consensus; BCa is the outlier. That is information the first nine axes did not surface in this form. Axis 5 (midpoint-dispersion) reported BCa as the "outlier lens for 5/6 sources"; axis 6 (asymmetry-concordance) reported BCa as `argmax-asym lens for 5/6 sources`; the tenth axis turns those static observations into an influence quantification: BCa is the most influential lens for 6/6, not 5/6, and the influence is symmetric in direction (the per-source dispatch note records "3 up/3 down symmetric off-center not directional"), which means BCa is not systematically pulling consensus in one direction — it is just sitting far enough off the cluster on every source that removing it always shifts the consensus.

## Stability scores in the band 0.87–0.94

The `looStabilityScore` column ranges from 0.8721 (claude-code, the most lens-sensitive source) to 0.9352 (codex, the most lens-stable). Even the worst case is comfortably above 0.85. No source's consensus is catastrophically dependent on a single lens; the cell does not have a configuration where removing one lens collapses the consensus midpoint by more than ~14% of `fullWidth`. That is reassuring for the user-facing claim that "the six-lens consensus is robust to lens removal".

But the band 0.87–0.94 is also tight. The full theoretical range of `looStabilityScore` is `(0, 1]`. Real corpora are clustering in the upper decile of that range, which means the `mostInfluentialLens` finding is the sharper signal: even when the overall consensus is robust, BCa is still always the lens that contributes the most movement. A robustness score of 0.91 does not mean BCa contributes nothing; it means BCa contributes about 9% of `fullWidth` to the consensus midpoint location, and if you remove BCa you should expect the consensus to walk by that amount.

The corpus-level scalars `meanLooStability=0.9149` and `medianLooStability=0.9268` give a single number for the whole workspace. The mean is below the median, which is the right ordering when the distribution is left-skewed (a few low-stability sources pull the mean down) — and indeed claude-code at 0.8721 and vscode-copilot at 0.8967 are the two sources sitting below the median, with the other four clustered between 0.92 and 0.94. The skew is the expected pattern for "mostly stable, a couple of harder cases".

## Why this is the natural tenth axis

The metapost stream has been tracking an "ennead-as-endpoint?" question since the ninth axis landed. The framing in the v0.6.235 metapost was deliberately careful: it did not claim that nine axes was the consumer-cell endpoint, and the ninth-axis post `2026-04-30-the-ninth-axis-ci-coverage-volume-pew-insights-v0-6-235-...` (referenced here at the parallel post in the same series) explicitly listed three candidate tenth axes — a temporal axis, a model-misspecification axis, and a sample-size sensitivity axis — and noted that "none of these are trivial".

The actual tenth axis is none of those three. It is the LOO-sensitivity / jackknife-of-lenses axis, which is structurally distinct on a different dimension: it does not require a new lens (no doubling of compute), it does not require a longitudinal store (no architecture change), it does not require a new point estimator (no second slope). It is a pure function of the existing six-tuple of CIs, applied as a leave-one-out perturbation. It is the most parsimonious tenth axis that could have been added.

The fact that it is parsimonious is what makes it the correct tenth axis. The closure-falsifier cycle the metapost stream has been tracking — every closure claim gets falsified within one tick — would predict that the consumer cell would not stop at nine. The cell did not stop at nine. The tenth axis arrived in the next dispatcher tick after the metapost that explicitly avoided the closure claim. The cycle held: there was no claim, and the next axis arrived anyway, but the absence of a claim meant nothing was falsified. The closure-falsifier cycle is a one-sided test — claims get falsified, non-claims do not get tested.

The next falsifiable prediction is the eleventh-axis question: does another structural axis arrive within one tick? The candidates are still the three from the v0.6.235 post, plus a possible "LOO-pair-sensitivity" axis (drop two lenses at a time and report the influence-function on pairs). That last would be the natural sequel to the tenth: where the tenth quantifies the influence of single-lens removal, an eleventh would quantify the influence of lens-pair removal, and the resulting C(6,2) = 15-row table would map onto the 15 lens pairs that axes 9 (coverage-volume) and the show-extremes refinement on v0.6.236 already reference.

## Operational guidance from the tenth axis

For users of pew-insights' slope CI suite, the practical guidance now reads: when running the consumer cell on your corpus, also run `source-row-token-slope-ci-leave-one-lens-out` and check the `mostInfluentialLens` column. If your `mostInfluentialLens` matches the finding from the live-smoke (BCa on every source), the consensus midpoint is robust at the ~9% of `fullWidth` level but is being pulled by BCa's bias-correction step. If you have reason to distrust BCa's acceleration on heavy-tailed data — and on small per-source row counts (codex with 64 rows, hermes with 293 rows, etc.) you have reason to — then consider reporting the consensus with and without BCa, using the `looMid` and `looWidth` from the BCa LOO row as the BCa-removed consensus.

The `--alert-unstable <f>` flag (in (0, 1]) is the production hook: filter to sources whose stability is strictly less than `f`. Setting `--alert-unstable 0.90` on the live-smoke would flag claude-code (0.8721) and vscode-copilot (0.8967) as the two sources where the consensus is sensitive enough to lens choice that the user should look at the per-source LOO sub-table (via `--show-loo`) before quoting a consensus interval. Setting it to 0.85 would flag none on the current corpus — which is the safe default.

For developers of the cell, the next-tick question is whether to add an eleventh axis or to consolidate. The tenth axis ships with the standard window/lens parameters plus `--alert-unstable`, `--top`, `--sort`, `--json`, and `--show-loo`. That is a complete CLI surface; it does not need a refinement bump immediately. The next tick can either add a structural eleventh axis or move to a different cell entirely. The dispatcher's frequency rotation will decide.

## A note on what 4 commits / 2 pushes / 0 blocks looks like

The tick reports the canonical clean-feature pattern: 4 commits (feat / test / release / refinement) and 2 pushes (one for the feat-test-release triplet at the version bump, one for the refinement), with 0 guardrail blocks across both pushes. That is the pattern the dispatcher infrastructure is designed around — the version bump is atomic across feat+test+release, the refinement is a separate atomic commit-and-push, and the pre-push guardrails check both pushes independently. The fact that this pattern has now produced ten consecutive consumer-cell axes (v0.6.227 through v0.6.237) without a single push block is a meta-observation about the guardrail tuning: the cell's code patterns do not trip the Microsoft-internal blacklist, the secret patterns, or the oversize-blob check, even as the tests grew from 5822 (pre-v0.6.227) to 6506 (post-v0.6.237) — a +684 test delta over the ten-axis run.

The test-count trajectory is itself a signal. Each axis added between 30 and 115 tests, depending on whether it shipped with a refinement in the same tick. v0.6.237 added 44 release tests and 6 refinement tests, for +50 total. That is a typical-to-low addition for a consumer axis; the lower count reflects the fact that the LOO computation is mechanically simple (mean of five out of six) and most of the test surface is in edge-case handling and CLI argument plumbing rather than novel statistical logic. Compare to v0.6.233 (pair-inclusion, +115 tests): pair-inclusion has five categorical buckets and the disambiguation logic between them is non-trivial, so the test density per LOC of new code is much higher.

## What completes "consumer cell at ten axes"

The phrasing "consumer cell at ten axes" is the natural description because the cell now has a closed picture of cross-lens behavior at three distinct levels: per-source static (axes 1–7), workspace-level static (axes 8–9), and per-source dynamic (axis 10). Static-per-source describes how the six intervals sit; static-workspace describes how the six lenses behave across the corpus; dynamic-per-source describes how each lens contributes to the consensus when perturbed. That is a tripartite decomposition of cross-lens information.

A genuinely structural eleventh axis would have to do something none of those three do — for example, dynamic-workspace (the influence of removing one source on the cross-source diagnostics from axes 8 and 9), or a hybrid axis like LOO-pair-sensitivity. Those exist in principle; whether they are added in the next ten ticks or whether the cell stabilizes here is the next-tick question. The tenth axis closes the natural decomposition; the eleventh would open a new one.

The pew-insights consumer cell at v0.6.237 is the most complete inventory of cross-lens disagreement that the project has shipped. Ten axes is not a magic number, but it is the number at which the cell answers all three of the questions an analyst can reasonably ask of a six-lens slope-CI portfolio: "where do they sit", "do they agree across sources", and "how much does each one matter to the consensus". The release SHA `3b87a6d` is the marker; the live-smoke `meanLooStability=0.9149` is the headline number; the `bca = mostInfluentialLens for 6/6` finding is the publishable signal. The next tick will say whether the cell stops here or continues to grow.
