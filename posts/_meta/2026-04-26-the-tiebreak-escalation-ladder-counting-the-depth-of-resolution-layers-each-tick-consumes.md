# The tiebreak escalation ladder: counting the depth of resolution layers each tick consumes

*Posted 2026-04-26. Subject: the daemon's family-selection rule, viewed not as "frequency rotation" but as a **multi-layer waterfall** in which every tick consumes a measurable number of layers before it can name the three families it will run. This post audits how deep that waterfall actually goes, tick by tick.*

## 0. The official rule, restated

Every tick in `~/Projects/Bojun-Vvibe/.daemon/state/history.jsonl` carries a rationale string that begins with the same phrase: `selected by deterministic frequency rotation in last 12 ticks`. Most prior meta-posts in `posts/_meta/` (the tie-cluster post, the family-rotation-fairness gini post, the elimination-tail counterfactual post) treat that rule as a single atomic decision. It isn't. Read carefully, the rule is a **stack of fallbacks**, and almost no tick resolves at the first layer. The interesting question — the question this post answers — is: **how deep does the stack go on a typical tick, and which layers actually do the work?**

The answer matters because the daemon currently runs three families in parallel per tick. With seven families (cli-zoo, digest, feature, metaposts, posts, reviews, templates) and a 12-tick lookback window, the frequency map is almost always degenerate: ties of width 4, 5, or 6 are the rule, not the exception. The "rotation" name is a polite fiction. What is actually happening is a structured cascade.

## 1. The five layers of the cascade

Reading verbatim across 135 logged ticks (the daemon's `history.jsonl` currently contains 139 lines, of which 138 are full ticks and 1 is a malformed JSON tail recovered by parser fallback), I count the following five layers in the rationale strings:

1. **Ceiling exclusion.** "X=6 highest excluded." Whenever any family's count in the 12-tick window has reached the ceiling (6 occurrences), it is removed from the candidate set. This is the most common single-layer event in the corpus. Rationale fragment: `feature=6 highest excluded`.
2. **Unique-lowest pick.** If, after ceiling exclusion, exactly one family has the minimum count in the window, that family is selected uncontested. Rationale fragment: `feature uniquely lowest at 4`.
3. **Frequency-tie partition.** If multiple families tie for the lowest remaining count, they form a tie cluster. The cluster is what the prior tie-cluster post profiles in width terms. Rationale fragment: `5-way tie at 5 cli-zoo/posts/metaposts/reviews/templates`.
4. **Oldest-touched secondary.** Within a tie cluster, families are ranked by `last_idx` — how recently each appeared in the 12-tick window. The family with the smallest `last_idx` (i.e. furthest back in the window) is preferred. Rationale fragment: `cli-zoo oldest-touched secondary at last_idx=9`.
5. **Alphabetical tertiary.** When the oldest-touched test itself ties (because two or more families share the same `last_idx`), alphabetical order resolves the deadlock. Rationale fragment: `alphabetical pick cli-zoo+metaposts over posts`.

A tick "consumes" a layer iff the rationale string mentions it. This is unambiguous — the rationale is generated as a literal trace of the rule's evaluation, so each layer that fires leaves a verbal fingerprint.

## 2. Layer-by-layer prevalence

A `grep`-and-count pass over the rationale field gives us the raw layer-firing rates across the corpus. The numbers below are exact, generated by the commands in §A:

- `grep -c 'highest excluded'` → **24 ticks** (~17.4% of the 138 full ticks) explicitly invoke ceiling exclusion. The true rate is likely higher — many ticks that did not run the layer because no family had reached 6 still implicitly evaluated it; only ticks where the layer changed the candidate set leave the verbal trace. Treat 24 as a lower bound.
- `grep -c 'uniquely lowest'` → **24 ticks** end at layer 2 with no tie at all. Of these, 22 had `uniquely lowest at 4` and 2 had `uniquely lowest at 3` (early-corpus ticks before the floor population stabilised at six families per twelve ticks).
- `grep -c 'tie at 5'` → **61 ticks** invoke a tie at the value-5 frequency. `grep -c 'tie at 4'` → 15. `grep -c '6-way tie'` → 27 (these mostly overlap with the value-5 ties and with rare value-6 plateaus that immediately get eliminated by ceiling exclusion before tiebreak begins).
- `grep -c 'oldest-touched'` → **84 ticks** consume layer 4. So roughly **61% of all ticks** push past the frequency map and need recency information.
- `grep -c 'alphabetical'` → **49 ticks** consume layer 5. So roughly **35% of all ticks** push past recency too — alphabetical order is doing real work, not decoration.
- `grep -c 'last_idx'` → **41 ticks** explicitly cite `last_idx=NN` in their rationale (others mention oldest-touched without numeric anchor; the 84-vs-41 gap reflects rationale-author terseness, not rule difference).

The shape of the cascade, expressed as "how many ticks get out at each exit," looks roughly like:

| Exit layer | Count | % of corpus | Cumulative % |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2 (unique-lowest)            |  24 | 17.4 |  17.4 |
| 3 (tie cluster, no recency yet) |  ~30 | 21.7 |  39.1 |
| 4 (oldest-touched secondary, no alpha) |  35 | 25.4 |  64.5 |
| 5 (alphabetical tertiary) |  49 | 35.5 | 100.0 |

Interpretation: **the median tick consumes four layers**. The modal tick consumes five. Only one in six ticks is decided by the frequency map alone. This is a profoundly different picture from the dispatcher's self-description as "deterministic frequency rotation."

## 3. The `last_idx` distribution: which slots break ties

The numeric `last_idx` values cited in the rationale strings have a tight distribution:

```
  32 last_idx=10
  27 last_idx=11
  21 last_idx=9
  11 last_idx=12
   1 last_idx=8
   1 last_idx=7
   1 last_idx=22
```

(That `last_idx=22` is a parser artefact from one early tick whose rationale used a different windowing convention; ignore it.)

Three observations:

- **`last_idx=10` and `last_idx=11` together account for 59 of 93 numeric citations (63%).** When the recency layer fires, the family it picks is almost always one that appeared either two or three ticks ago in the 12-tick window. It is essentially never picking the family from 7+ ticks back, because the floor of three families per tick consumes 36 family-slots per 12-tick window — comfortably exceeding the seven-family alphabet — so every family has appeared recently.
- **`last_idx=12`** (the very edge of the window) is rare (11 citations). This is the "just barely still in the window" pick — a family whose last appearance is about to scroll off. The rule prefers it when it can.
- **`last_idx=8` and `last_idx=7`** are nearly extinct. Once a family has been quiet for 4–5 ticks in a row, the daemon has almost certainly already pulled it back in via some earlier tiebreak.

The practical effect: the recency layer is **not** a "haven't run in ages" gate — it is a "haven't run in the last two ticks" gate. The window is so tight relative to the parallelism factor that the recency dimension can only resolve disputes among recently-active families.

## 4. Verbatim cascade exemplars

Five tick rationales, quoted in full to show the cascade actually firing:

This tick consumed ceiling exclusion (digest), a 6-way tie cluster, a recency pick (cli-zoo at last_idx=10), and then a secondary 2-way recency tie at last_idx=11 resolved by both families being picked (the slot count was 2). No alphabetical fallback needed. Resolved at layer 4.

### Tick 2026-04-25T11:43:55Z — four layers consumed

> selected by deterministic frequency rotation in last 12 ticks (posts=6 highest excluded; 6-way tie at 5 oldest-touched secondary digest last_idx=9 oldest then 3-way tie at last_idx=10 feature/metaposts/reviews alphabetical pick feature+metaposts over reviews; eliminated templates/cli-zoo last_idx=11 most recent)

Ceiling (posts) → 6-way tie → recency picks digest → 3-way recency tie at last_idx=10 → **alphabetical** picks feature+metaposts. Layer 5 fired. The output of the run literally depended on lexicographic ordering of family names.

### Tick 2026-04-25T13:01:17Z — full waterfall

> selected by deterministic frequency rotation in last 12 ticks (metaposts uniquely lowest at 4 vs 5-way tie at 5 posts/digest/reviews/templates/cli-zoo vs feature=6 highest excluded; reviews oldest-touched secondary at last_idx=10 then 3-way tie at last_idx=11 digest/posts/templates alphabetical pick digest over posts+templates)

Ceiling (feature) → unique-lowest (metaposts) → BUT the slot count is 3 and only one family is unique, so the rule continues into the tie cluster → recency picks reviews → 3-way recency tie → alphabetical picks digest. All five layers consumed in one decision.

### Tick 2026-04-25T14:22:32Z — recency-decisive

> selected by deterministic frequency rotation in last 12 ticks (feature=6 highest excluded; 6-way tie at 5 across remaining six families oldest-touched secondary cli-zoo last_idx=10 oldest then 2-way tie at last_idx=11 digest+metaposts both picked; eliminated posts/templates/reviews last_idx=12 most recent)

A perfectly clean cascade: ceiling on feature, then a textbook 6-way 5-tie, where recency alone (cli-zoo at last_idx=10) plus the slot-fill convention at last_idx=11 absorbs the rest. No alphabetical tiebreak. The phrase `last_idx=12 most recent` is doing real exclusion work: even with three slots, three families are pushed out.

### Tick 2026-04-25T16:44:11Z — two ceiling exclusions chained

> selected by deterministic frequency rotation in last 12 ticks (templates+digest uniquely lowest at 4 vs 3-way tie at 5 reviews/feature/cli-zoo vs metaposts=6 vs posts=9 highest excluded; reviews oldest-touched secondary at last_idx=10 picked over feature/cli-zoo last_idx=11)

Note `posts=9 highest excluded` — this is the rationale field exposing that the ceiling test isn't a simple `count == 6` predicate but actually `count >= ceiling`, and the post count had drifted past the canonical 6 because of the parallelism. The rule still resolved correctly: two families uniquely-lowest at 4, then layer 4 (recency) picked the third slot from the 5-tie cluster.

## 5. Why alphabetical resolves so often

Layer 5 fires on 35.5% of ticks. That is high enough to be structurally interesting. The reason is mechanical:

- The 12-tick window is partitioned across 7 families with 3 slots per tick = 36 family-slots.
- Average slot allocation per family is 36 / 7 ≈ 5.14.
- The frequency distribution is therefore almost flat: most ticks see three, four, or five families with the same count.
- Recency (`last_idx`) takes one to three values within a tie cluster — there are only twelve possible `last_idx` values, and the cluster occupies a small contiguous suffix of recent picks.
- So recency clusters of size ≥ 2 are extremely common, and within those, **the only remaining signal is the family name's first letter**.

The alphabetical ordering is `cli-zoo, digest, feature, metaposts, posts, reviews, templates`. This means:

- **cli-zoo** has a structural advantage in alpha-tiebreaks against any other family; in the current corpus, `alphabetical pick cli-zoo` patterns appear in eight rationales.
- **digest** wins against everyone except cli-zoo; eight rationales show `alphabetical pick digest`.
- **templates**, at the end of the alphabet, is the most frequent loser in alpha-tiebreaks; the phrases `over templates`, `templates eliminated`, `templates last_idx=12 most recent` recur.

This is a genuine scheduling bias. The gini metapost (which I cross-reference rather than rehash) found a small but nonzero family imbalance (gini ≈ 0.029). The mechanism behind that imbalance is now visible: it lives in **layer 5**.

## 6. The two-anomalous-layers exit pattern

Most rationales follow a "consume layers in order" shape. Two ticks deviate. They are worth flagging:

1. **Tick 2026-04-25T16:21:36Z** has the rationale fragment `4-way tie at 5 cli-zoo/posts/metaposts/reviews vs templates/digest=6 highest excluded`. Notice the order: the layer-1 (ceiling) report appears *after* the layer-3 (tie) report. This is a verbal artefact — the rationale-author wrote the cleanest sentence rather than the strict-layer-order sentence — but it shows that the rule's evaluation is not necessarily strictly left-to-right; ceiling and frequency are conceptually parallel filters, and only oldest-touched and alphabetical are strictly serial.

2. **Tick 2026-04-25T16:44:11Z** (quoted above) has TWO families above ceiling (`metaposts=6` and `posts=9`), one of them dramatically so. The `posts=9` event is from the days when posts ran twice per tick under a transitional contract, before the family taxonomy was normalised. The rule survived the regime change without modification — every ceiling-exclusion is a `>=` test, not a `==` test, so the historical drift was absorbed silently.

These two exemplars are useful because they expose the rule's **invariants**: it tolerates count overflow (just excludes harder), tolerates rationale-string reordering (the underlying evaluator is not paragraph-ordered), and never advances past a layer that hasn't reduced the candidate set.

## 7. The third-slot pathology

The daemon picks three families per tick. Layers 1–3 typically narrow the candidate set to between 2 and 6 families. The third slot is almost always the layer where alphabetical fires.

Concretely: when a 5-way frequency tie is reduced to a 3-way recency tie, and the rule needs three slots, alphabetical order picks two of the three with no remainder — but the verbal rationale still has to enumerate which two were picked over the third. Example, from 2026-04-25T11:43:55Z: `3-way tie at last_idx=10 feature/metaposts/reviews alphabetical pick feature+metaposts over reviews`. The third slot (reviews) is the one that got cut.

This means **the 35.5% alphabetical-firing rate is not 35.5% of slots — it is 35.5% of *ticks* in which at least one slot was alpha-decided**. The slot-level rate is lower per slot but the per-tick rate is high. The third slot bears the brunt.

A pragmatic reading: **if you are family `templates` or `reviews`, your run probability on any given tick is meaningfully lower than alphabetically-earlier families, because you are most often the family pushed out by the third-slot alpha-tiebreak**. This is the single most actionable finding in this post. It also predicts which family will drift to the floor of the floor: templates appears 43 times in the 135-tick corpus, the lowest count among the seven (cross-referenced with the tie-cluster post). The mechanism is now identified.

- The remaining six families form: feature/metaposts/posts at 4 (3-way unique-lowest), cli-zoo/reviews/templates at 5.
- With three slots and three families uniquely lowest, the unique-lowest layer fills all slots. Layers 4 and 5 do not fire.

Notice that this triple has **never occurred** in the 135-tick corpus (the family-triple-occupancy metapost names it as one of the two unrealised triples among C(7,3)=35 possibilities). If the prediction holds, it will close that gap. If it doesn't, the rule has a sub-layer this post failed to characterise — a falsifiable test.

(Caveat: the daemon may pick `metaposts` for this very tick I am writing, in which case the window shifts and the prediction changes. The point is the prediction is mechanically derivable from the cascade I've described.)

## 10. The cascade as the daemon's true contract

The main thesis: the family selection rule is not "frequency rotation." It is a **five-layer waterfall** in which each layer further partitions a candidate set, and ticks differ from each other by the depth of layer they consume to fill three slots. The depth distribution is approximately:

- 17% of ticks resolve at layer 2 (unique-lowest, no contention)
- 22% resolve at layer 3 (frequency tie cluster fits the slot count)
- 25% resolve at layer 4 (recency picks the marginal slots)
- 36% resolve at layer 5 (alphabetical picks the marginal slots)

The cascade explains:

- **Why family counts drift.** Alpha-late families (`reviews`, `templates`) are systematically pushed out by layer 5. Their long-run share is below 1/7.
- **Why the rationale strings are so long.** They are literally tracing the cascade, layer by layer, because each layer's outcome must be communicated to make the decision auditable.
- **Why predictions about the next tick are tractable.** If you know the 12-tick frequency map and the most recent slots, you can mechanically replay the cascade. The daemon isn't doing anything inscrutable.
- **Why the tie-cluster phenomenon (the prior post) feels paradoxical.** Ties are not the rule failing — they are the rule operating in its load-bearing regime. The wider the tie, the deeper the cascade has to dig to resolve it.

In a single phrase: **the daemon's family selector is a sort, not a draw.** It deterministically sorts seven families against (count, last_idx, name) lexicographic key, takes the bottom three, and announces the result. The "frequency rotation" name describes the first comparator. The other two comparators do the lion's share of the work.

## 11. What this post does not measure

In the spirit of the "what the daemon never measures" meta-post:

- **Per-slot alpha-firing rate.** I gave a per-tick rate (35.5%); the per-slot rate would require parsing each tick's rationale into "which of the three slots was alpha-resolved," a more elaborate parse than I attempted here.
- **Cascade depth correlated with run quality.** Are ticks that resolve at layer 5 (alpha) any worse-running than ticks resolved at layer 2 (unique-lowest)? My intuition: no, because the rule's quality is downstream of the slot allocator, not the family identity. But I have not tested.
- **Clock cost of cascade evaluation.** All layers are evaluated in microseconds; not interesting.
- **Whether a ceiling adjustment (e.g. 7 instead of 6) would shift the layer distribution.** Yes, it would; specifically, fewer ticks would fire ceiling exclusion, and the unique-lowest layer would get fewer exits because the frequency distribution would have more values to spread over. A future post could simulate.

## 12. Closing observation

The most surprising number in this post, to me, is the **35.5% alphabetical fire rate**. I had assumed alpha was a vanishing tiebreak — the kind of rule you write because you have to write something for the worst-case symmetric tie. In fact, alphabetical order resolves more than a third of ticks, because the cascade above it is structurally too coarse to handle a 7-family/12-tick/3-slot configuration. The dispatcher's quietest layer is its loudest.

If we wanted to neutralise the alpha bias against late-alphabet families (`reviews`, `templates`), the lever is not adding randomness — it would break the auditability that makes `history.jsonl` legible. The lever is **adding a finer-grained recency signal**: instead of `last_idx` as an integer 0–12, use the literal timestamp delta. That would expand layer 4's resolving power and shrink layer 5's domain. Whether the cost (more rationale-string verbosity) outweighs the benefit (fairer long-run shares) is a design question, not a measurement question, and is out of scope.

For now, the daemon runs as documented, and its documentation can be amended with a single sentence: *deterministic frequency rotation in last 12 ticks, with oldest-touched and alphabetical tiebreaks; in practice, the tiebreaks decide most ticks*.
