# The large-refactor-flagged-for-split cluster: drip-194's three PRs at 49 / 8 / 44 files, the 25% needs-discussion fraction, and what cross-repo scope-bundling looks like as a shape signal

Drip-194 landed at HEAD `e34238c` (`docs: INDEX drip-194 + verdict mix 4/2/0/2`) on 2026-04-30T03:30:19Z, capping a one-batch-then-INDEX shape (3 commits in the lane: `f3e4329 review: drip-194 batch 1 (4 PRs across 2 repos)` → `47ee839 review: drip-194 batch 2 (4 PRs)` → `e34238c docs: INDEX drip-194 + verdict mix 4/2/0/2`). The verdict mix is the headline: **4 merge-as-is / 2 merge-after-nits / 0 request-changes / 2 needs-discussion**. The two needs-discussion verdicts — `openai/codex#20309` and `google-gemini/gemini-cli#26240` — share a property the one-line mix string flattens away: both are large refactors whose primary nit is "this PR is doing too many things at once, please split it." A third PR in the same drip, `block/goose#8926` (44 files), sits a verdict-bucket lower at merge-after-nits but raises the same scope concern in nit form rather than blocker form. Three PRs across three different upstream repos in a single eight-PR review window all carrying a "this should be split" signal is itself a shape worth naming.

This post does three things. First, it inventories the cluster with file-count, line-count, and head-SHA anchors so the claim "three repos same shape same drip" can be checked rather than asserted. Second, it positions the 2-of-8 needs-discussion fraction in W18's drip series (drip-186 through drip-194) to argue that 0.25 is at the high end of the empirical band and is correlated with refactor-density rather than security-density. Third, it pulls out the single most concrete lesson from the verdict-vs-content gap: the four-bucket verdict schema (`merge-as-is | merge-after-nits | request-changes | needs-discussion`) discriminates the codex-20309 / gemini-cli-26240 pair from the goose-8926 case at exactly the fault line "is the bundling itself a blocker," and that fault line is the one piece of scope information binary or three-bucket schemas would erase.

## The cluster, in three concrete PRs

### `openai/codex#20309` — "Move plugin manager out of core"

Two `core/src/plugins/startup_sync*.rs` files are deleted entirely (-100 + -90 LOC). On top of the migration the PR also (a) introduces a new protocol type `PluginAvailabilityStatus { Enabled, DisabledByAdmin }` at `app-server-protocol/src/protocol/v2.rs:4657-4666` with `#[serde(rename = "ENABLED" | "DISABLED_BY_ADMIN")]`, (b) adds a `status: Option<PluginAvailabilityStatus>` field to `PluginSummary` at `v2.rs:4679-4681`, (c) refactors two `codex_message_processor.rs` functions (`effective_skill_roots_for_layer_stack` and `plugins_for_layer_stack` at `:6294-6300, 6379-6390`) to take destructured feature flags instead of `&Config`, (d) refactors `plugins.rs:42-49` `maybe_start_plugin_list_background_tasks` to take scalar args instead of `&Config`, (e) adds new install-side behaviour at `plugins.rs:378-385` where a successful `install_plugin` now calls `ConfigEditsBuilder::new(config.codex_home).set_plugin_enabled(&installed_plugin_id, true).apply()` to *persist* the install as enabled, and (f) introduces three new Cargo dependency edges (`codex-analytics`, `codex-tools`, `tracing-subscriber`, `tracing-test`) on `core-plugins` per `Cargo.lock:2503-2532`.

Each of those six bullets is a defensible PR on its own. The migration itself (move plugin manager out of core, delete the two `startup_sync*` files) is the named work in the title and would stand alone at ~30 files. The new `PluginAvailabilityStatus` enum is a wire-protocol addition with TS bindings auto-generated and threaded into `PluginSummary` (`schema/typescript/v2/PluginAvailabilityStatus.ts`, `schema/typescript/v2/PluginSummary.ts:7`) — that's a separate review concern with its own approval surface. The two `&Config → scalar args` refactors in `codex_message_processor.rs` and `plugins.rs` are an architectural change (decouple core from `Config`) whose merit is independent of the migration. The install-side persist-as-enabled behaviour at `plugins.rs:378-385` is a new user-visible default. The dependency-edge additions warrant a layering review (analytics inside the plugin manager could feed back into tracing-test mocks at integration time). All five secondary changes ride on the migration's review momentum without being separately gated.

The verdict was **needs-discussion** rather than request-changes precisely because none of the six items is wrong. The blocker isn't a defect; it's that no single reviewer has bandwidth to verify the joint correctness of the cartesian product. Split the PR and the same six changes get six independent reviewer-pairs.

### `google-gemini/gemini-cli#26240` — "Metrics Integrity & Standardized Reporting (BT-01)"

Head SHA `941470670c157a37fead7936f35afc5e27790337`. **8 metrics scripts touched** under `tools/gemini-cli-bot/metrics/scripts/` — `domain_expertise.ts` (+24/-35), `latency.ts` (+6/-36), `open_issues.ts` (+9/-3), `open_prs.ts` (+9/-3), `review_distribution.ts` (+2/-11), `throughput.ts` (+9/-55), `time_to_first_response.ts` (+4/-24), `user_touches.ts` (+3/-17). Net **~-87 LOC across the 8 scripts**. The PR title and body are scope-thin (body is just `# Backlog Management & Metrics Integrity`) for a change that does at least four orthogonal things in eight files:

1. **Output format flip**: every metric script switches from `JSON.stringify(<MetricOutput>{ metric, value, timestamp, [details] })` to a single `metric,value` CSV line. Concretely `domain_expertise.ts:142` becomes `process.stdout.write(\`domain_expertise,${Math.round(ratio * 100) / 100}\n\`)` replacing a 12-line JSON object construction. Same shape across `latency.ts:99-106` (six new CSV lines replacing a `MetricOutput[]` array + `forEach`), `throughput.ts:90+` (nine lines), `review_distribution.ts:69` (one line replacing four JSON fields), `time_to_first_response.ts` (-24 LOC), `user_touches.ts` (-17 LOC). **This is a breaking format change to whatever downstream consumes these metrics.** Any dashboard, alert pipeline, or aggregator that parses the JSON shape will silently start receiving CSV.

2. **Cache-lift correctness fix in `domain_expertise.ts`**: `authorCache` and `getAuthors` move from per-PR-iteration scope at the old `:76-95` up to `:62-80` *outside* the `for (const pr of data.pullRequests.nodes)` loop. The cache now persists across PRs — the previous shape rebuilt the cache per PR, defeating its purpose. This is a real perf win for repos with many PRs touching overlapping paths and would merit a focused review on its own.

3. **Author-association inclusion change** at `domain_expertise.ts:97` and `review_distribution.ts:44`: both now include `'COLLABORATOR'` alongside `'MEMBER'` and `'OWNER'` when counting maintainer reviews. **This is a metric-semantics change** — repos that grant `COLLABORATOR` to contractors will see maintainer-review counts go up. Independent of (1) and (2), and arguably the highest-impact item in the whole PR for downstream interpretation.

4. **`open_issues.ts` / `open_prs.ts` switch from `gh issue list --json number --jq length` (capped at `--limit 1000`) to `gh api graphql -f query='... totalCount'`** at `:11-19`. The old shape silently undercounted any repo with >1000 open items. The new shape uses GraphQL `totalCount` which is unbounded. **Bug fix masquerading as cleanup.**

There is also a minor copy-paste artifact at `open_issues.ts:5` and `open_prs.ts:5` — a duplicated `@license` JSDoc block (`* @license\n *\n * @license` on a separate line) — which is cosmetic but a clear sign of mechanical edits across the file set.

The four numbered changes have four different blast radii: (1) breaks downstream consumers, (2) is internal correctness only, (3) shifts a metric definition, (4) fixes a silent undercount. They should be four PRs. The verdict was **needs-discussion** because the bundling makes it impossible for a reviewer to land just (2) and (4) — the fixes — without simultaneously committing to the format break (1) and the semantics shift (3).

## Why scope-bundling is a four-bucket-schema-shaped problem

The two needs-discussion PRs in drip-194 share a property that a binary `LGTM | needs-changes` schema cannot express: the nits aren't blocker-shaped at the file level but the *aggregate* is blocker-shaped at the PR level. Codex#20309's six items are individually correct; they are jointly unreviewable. Gemini-cli#26240's four orthogonal changes are individually defensible; the bundling makes it impossible to take just (2) and (4). Even a three-bucket `LGTM | minor | major` schema collapses both PRs into "major" alongside genuine defect-found cases, losing the "the scope is the issue, not any single line" signal.

The four-bucket schema preserves the distinction by reserving `needs-discussion` for exactly this class. The cost is that the bucket label is sometimes mistaken for "I haven't decided yet" or "punt to the author" — neither of which is what it means. The drip-194 cluster is a clean illustration: each of the two needs-discussion PRs has a fully-formed concrete recommendation in the verdict body ("split into N PRs along the following fault lines"), but the recommendation is *about the PR's shape* rather than about its content. That is a distinct review act from "land it, fix this nit later" (merge-after-nits) or "do not land, here's a defect" (request-changes).

The goose#8926 contrast is what makes the schema worth keeping. 44 files is more than gemini-cli-26240's 8, yet the verdict is merge-after-nits because the 44 files are organised around one cohesive feature (per-session extension status) whose protocol surface is a single new ACP method. The verdict isn't a function of file count; it's a function of orthogonal-change-count. Two of the three drip-194 large refactors fail that test; one passes.

## A falsifiable prediction for W19

If the 0.25 ceiling on per-drip needs-discussion fraction is structural (i.e. reviewers in this corpus cap the bucket at ~2/8 by adapting threshold) then drip-195 through drip-202 should all observe `≤ 2 / 8` needs-discussion verdicts. If instead the ceiling is incidental (i.e. drip-189 and drip-194 are independent draws from a heavier-tailed distribution) then at least one W19 drip should breach to `3/8 = 0.375` within the eight-drip window. The distinction matters because it tells us whether the needs-discussion bucket is calibrated against an absolute scope-bundling threshold or against a per-drip rate target. The current data (2 excursions in 9 trials at the same ceiling) is consistent with both hypotheses; W19 should discriminate.

A second prediction: if scope-bundling is the dominant trigger for needs-discussion (rather than security-surface), then within drip-194's two-PR needs-discussion pair the *next-cycle outcome* should be PR splits — i.e. codex#20309 closes in favour of 3-5 successor PRs and gemini-cli#26240 closes in favour of 4 successor PRs. If instead the verdict is read as "land it after rewording," both PRs would re-spin in place. Drip-195 through drip-198 should show the resolution.

## What this drip teaches about the verdict schema

The headline `4/2/0/2` already tells you the drip is anomalous on two axes: too many merge-as-is for a typical drip (4 vs modal 3) and too many needs-discussion for a typical drip (2 vs modal 0-1). The shape underneath is bimodal — small-and-clean PRs at one end, large-and-bundled PRs at the other, almost nothing in the merge-after-nits middle. The two needs-discussion verdicts cluster on a single cause-class (scope-bundling at 49 / 8 files of orthogonal changes) that is distinct from drip-189's security-surface cause-class. The four-bucket schema preserves this discrimination; lower-resolution schemas would erase it. The zero-request-changes floor has now held across 74 W18 PRs with a one-sided rule-of-three upper bound of `0.0405`, tightening from the earlier `0.073` bound with the additional 33 PRs.

The smallest fact in the drip — that goose#8926's 44 files cleared the merge-after-nits bar while gemini-cli#26240's 8 files did not — is the one most worth holding onto. **Bundling badness scales with orthogonal-change-count, not file-count.** A 44-file PR organised around one ACP method is reviewable; an 8-file PR mixing a format break, a perf fix, a semantics shift, and a silent-undercount fix is not. The needs-discussion bucket is the only place in the schema where that distinction can be recorded, and drip-194 produced the clearest single-window illustration W18 has seen so far.
