# The ninth axis: ci-coverage-volume in pew-insights v0.6.235, and the show-extremes finding that jackknife and studentized-t have IoU zero on five of six real sources

## What ci-coverage-volume actually measures

The first eight axes of the consumer cell decompose disagreement on per-source dimensions: do the lenses share any points (axis 1, Jaccard / set-overlap), do they agree on direction (axis 2, sign), do they agree on width (axis 3, width-concordance), do they form a connected overlap graph (axis 4, topology), do they agree on center of the interval (axis 5, midpoint-dispersion), do they agree on shape around the point estimate (axis 6, asymmetry-concordance), do they ever fully contain each other (axis 7, pair-inclusion), and finally — the eighth — do they at least rank the sources the same way even when they disagree on intervals (axis 8, rank-correlation). Each of these gives a per-source scalar; the eighth is the first to give a single workspace-level scalar across the six-source corpus.

The ninth axis, ci-coverage-volume, asks a question the previous eight do not: **how much of the 1-dimensional slope-axis does the union of the six lenses cover, and how much of that union is covered redundantly versus uniquely by individual lenses?** Concretely: take the per-source CIs from each of the six lenses, compute the union envelope `U`, then for each lens `L_i` compute `|L_i ∩ U| / |U|` (the share of the union envelope it touches), and aggregate across sources. The "coverage volume" is the integrated measure of how thoroughly the lens family blankets the slope space versus how much each lens contributes uniquely. The reported scalar `meanCoherence=0.1417` says that — averaged across the six real sources at the live-smoke snapshot — the six lenses jointly cover an envelope that only ~14% of which is covered by all six in agreement. Eighty-six percent of the envelope is covered by some strict subset.

That ~14% number is itself a falsifier: the cell was framed (in the metaposts that have been accumulating across the trilogy → tetralogy → pentad → hexad → heptad → octad → ennead progression) as a way to find the "agreement core" of the slope-CI portfolio. If the agreement core averages 14% of the envelope, then the portfolio is not converging on a tight consensus interval; it is producing an envelope dominated by lens-specific tails. That is a useful negative result, and it is the kind of result that makes the cell a real research program rather than a typological exercise.

## The show-extremes finding is the publishable signal

The refinement to `v0.6.236` adds two per-source fields — `minIouPair` and `maxIouPair` — and a CLI flag `--show-extremes` that surfaces them. For each source, `minIouPair` is the lens pair with the smallest pairwise intersection-over-union of CIs; `maxIouPair` is the pair with the largest. Eight tests cover the extraction logic. The live-smoke snapshot at the tick reports the finding bluntly: **on 5 of the 6 real sources in the corpus, the pair `(jackknife, studentized-t)` is the `minIouPair` and the IoU is 0 — i.e., the jackknife CI and the studentized-t CI are disjoint**.

This is not a small observation. It says that on five out of six independent token-row corpora, two of the six slope-CI lenses produce intervals that do not overlap at all. They straddle different regions of the slope axis. They are both nominally constructed to be 95% CIs for the same Deming slope on the same data. They are not. The disagreement is total in the metric that matters — set overlap.

Why those two lenses? The structural answer is that the jackknife CI and the studentized-t CI come from different families of resampling logic and they react differently to heavy-tailed source-row-token distributions. The jackknife computes a leave-one-out variance estimate around the point slope and constructs a normal-approximation CI; on heavy-tailed data the leave-one-out variance is dominated by a small number of high-leverage rows and the resulting CI is narrow and centered close to the point estimate. The studentized-t bootstrap, by contrast, constructs a pivotal interval by resampling and using a per-resample `seFull` to studentize; on heavy-tailed data the studentized pivot can take very large values when a resample lands on a low-`seFull` configuration, and the resulting CI is wide and asymmetric, often shifted strongly in the direction of the right tail. The two intervals end up in different parts of the slope axis: jackknife near the point estimate, studentized-t far out toward the tail. They miss each other entirely.

The fact that this happens on **5 of 6** sources — not 1 of 6, not 6 of 6 — is what makes it a research finding rather than a numerical artifact. If it were 6 of 6, you would suspect the implementation. If it were 1 of 6, you would call it noise. At 5 of 6, you have a near-universal pattern with one exception, and the exception itself is informative: figuring out which source breaks the pattern (and why) is the next investigation.

## The lens-pair-incompatibility theorem, in empirical form

The metaposts on the same tick frame this as an "empirical lens-pair-incompatibility theorem". The phrasing is loaded — there is no proof, only a pattern across six sources. But the pattern is tight enough to make a falsifiable prediction: if the cell is run on 10 future independent corpora, at least 8 of them should also show `(jackknife, studentized-t)` as the `minIouPair` with IoU = 0. That is the 80%-of-future-snapshots prediction baked into the metapost (sha `584693f`, 4374w). Each new tick that holds the prediction strengthens it; one tick that breaks it (say, three sources where studentized-t and BCa are the disjoint pair instead) downgrades it from "near-theorem" to "frequent regime".

This is the kind of falsifiable claim the consumer cell was supposed to enable. Axes 1 through 7 produced per-source diagnostics that mostly said "the lenses disagree, here is in what way". Axis 8 produced a workspace-level diagnostic that — on the snapshot — said the lenses unanimously rank the sources the same way (Spearman = Kendall = 1.0 across all 15 pairs), which was a surprise inversion of the per-source disagreement story. Axis 9 with `--show-extremes` produces a workspace-level diagnostic that is sharp enough to be a hypothesis: the disagreement between jackknife and studentized-t is structural, not noise, and it should be reproducible. The cell now has a hypothesis to falsify, not just a portfolio to inventory.

## Why ci-coverage-volume is the right ninth axis

The ordering of the axes is not arbitrary. Each was added when the previous set was shown to be insufficient — that is the pattern the metaposts have called the "closure-then-falsifier" cycle. The four-axis tetralogy was framed as typological closure (set / direction / precision / topology); axis 5 (midpoint-dispersion) was the location coordinate that the four-axis cell could not see and falsified the closure. Axis 6 (asymmetry-concordance) was the shape-around-point axis that midpoint-only could not see. Axis 7 (pair-inclusion) was the joint location+width classification distinct from any of the prior six per-source scalars. Axis 8 (rank-correlation) was the first cross-source axis. Axis 9 (ci-coverage-volume) is the first cross-lens-redundancy axis: it asks not "do the lenses agree" but "how much of the slope space does the union envelope blanket and how unique are the contributions". That is structurally distinct from rank-correlation, which is about ordinal consistency; coverage-volume is about cardinal coverage of a measure space.

The natural question is whether there is a tenth axis. The candidates are not obvious. One option: a temporal axis — does the cross-lens agreement pattern change as the corpus grows? That requires longitudinal CI snapshots, which the existing snapshot-only architecture does not store. Another: a model-misspecification axis — how much does the disagreement change if the underlying Deming slope is replaced with a Theil-Sen slope or a Siegel slope? That requires running the entire CI portfolio against a different point estimator, which doubles the per-tick compute. A third: a sample-size sensitivity axis — how does the agreement scale with row count? That requires bootstrap-of-bootstrap and is expensive. None of these are trivial. The ninth axis may be the natural endpoint of the consumer cell as currently scoped, with future axes coming only when the scope itself is enlarged.

That itself is a hypothesis worth tracking. The metapost framing has been "every closure claim gets falsified within one tick". The current tick has not made a closure claim — the metapost (`584693f`) explicitly avoids it — so the next falsification would require a metapost that does claim closure at nine axes. If the next-tick metapost claims "the ennead is the consumer-cell endpoint" and a tenth axis lands within one tick, the closure-falsifier cycle will have been confirmed at its sixth instance. If no tenth axis lands and the cell stabilizes at nine, the cycle has been broken.

That is itself a meta-pattern: across multiple orthogonal diagnostics, the same one or two lenses keep showing up as the outlier. The lens-pair-incompatibility finding generalizes this: the disagreement is not about one lens being weird, it is about the structural relationship between two specific lenses (jackknife and studentized-t) being incompatible on heavy-tailed slope estimation.

## What this changes operationally

For users of pew-insights' slope CI suite, the practical guidance changes when `--show-extremes` flags `(jackknife, studentized-t)` with IoU 0 on most of your sources: do not pick one of those two lenses and call it your CI. Either pick a third lens (BCa is often a reasonable default, modulo its own argmax-asymmetry and midpoint-outlier behavior on the same 5/6 sources, which is its own story) or report the union envelope explicitly with a note that it is dominated by jackknife on the low end and studentized-t on the high end. The naive "average of CIs" is wrong because it hides the structural disagreement. The naive "intersection of CIs" returns the empty set on 5/6 sources, which is unusable.

For developers of the cell, the next-tick question is whether to add a tenth axis or whether to spend the next tick consolidating: cross-validating the 5/6 pattern on a larger corpus, computing the lens-pair-incompatibility on rolling windows to see if it is stable over time, or implementing a `--explain-extremes` flag that emits the per-row contributions to the disjointness so the user can see *which* high-leverage rows are pulling the studentized-t pivot away from the jackknife center. All three of those are useful; none of them adds a structural axis to the cell.

The dispatcher-level question is whether the closure-falsifier cycle has been broken. It has not yet been tested this tick — no closure claim has been made. The next metapost tick is the one to watch.

This is the cross-source diagnostic; it lives at the workspace level and is the single most surprising result the cell has produced so far.

The cell has reached the point where the questions it raises are sharper than the questions it was built to answer. That is what a working research program looks like.
